Lecture 1: August 3, 2023 - Introduction & Rutherford Scattering

Lecture 1: August 3, 2023, Thursday

  • Thomson's Plum-Pudding Model:

    • Description: Assumed a spherical distribution of positively charged particles uniformly distributed throughout a sphere, with electrons embedded within it to make the atom net neutral.
    • Status: Eventually proved wrong by experimental evidence.

  • Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment (Geiger & Marsden Experiment):

    • Setup: A beam of fast-moving alpha ($\alpha$) particles was directed at a thin gold (Au) foil.
      • An $\alpha$-particle is a helium nucleus ($^4\text{He}_2$), consisting of two protons and two neutrons, thus carrying a charge of $+2e$.
    • Observation:
      • Most $\alpha$-particles passed straight through the foil with little or no deflection.
      • A small fraction of $\alpha$-particles (approximately 1 out of 8000) were scattered at large angles, some even deflecting by more than 90Â° (back-scattered).
    • Implication:
      • This observation contradicted Thomson's model, which predicted only small deflections.
      • It implied that the positive charge and most of the atom's mass must be concentrated in a very small, dense region at the center, which was named the nucleus.
    • Origin of Alpha Particles: $\alpha$-particles are typically obtained from the natural radioactive decay of heavy elements like Uranium ($\text{U}$) and Radium ($\text{Ra}$).
      • Example Decay: For instance, the decay of Uranium-235: $$ ^{235}_{92}\text{U} \rightarrow ^{231}_{Z}\text{X} + ^4_2\text{He} \quad (\alpha\text{-particle}) $$ Where $Z = 92 - 2 = 90$.
    • Energy Considerations:
      • The notes mention "a distribution of energies for decay into more than 2 species," which is a general comment on complex decays.
      • However, for a two-body decay (like $\alpha$-decay), the $\alpha$-particles should have a fixed energy. Typical energies for $\alpha$-particles from natural decay are around $5 \text{ MeV}$.

  • Interaction between Alpha Particles and the Nucleus:

    • The scattering observed is primarily due to the Coulombic (electrostatic) interaction between the positively charged $\alpha$-particle and the positively charged nucleus.
    • The mass of the positively charged nucleus is significantly greater than the mass of the negatively charged electrons (the notes state "2000 times heavier").
      • Specifically, an $\alpha$-particle has a mass roughly $4 \times m_p \approx 7400 m_e$. For a gold nucleus ($Z=79$), its mass is $197 \text{ amu}$, roughly $197 \times 1836 m_e \approx 3.6 \times 10^5 m_e$.
    • Therefore, the deflection of the $\alpha$-particle is dominated by its interaction with the massive nucleus; the electrons' contribution to the deflection is negligible.
    • Gold (Au) Properties:
      • Crystal Structure: Face-Centered Cubic (FCC).
      • Density: $\rho \approx 19.32 \text{ g/cm}^3$.
      • Molar Mass: $M \approx 197 \text{ g/mol}$.
      • Charge of Gold Nucleus: $Q_p = 79e = 79 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.
      • Charge of Alpha Particle: $Q_\alpha = 2e = 2 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.
      • Atomic Radius: $R_{\text{Au}} \approx 1.44 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$.

  • FCC Lattice and Packing Fraction:

    • For an FCC lattice, the relationship between the lattice constant ($a$) and the atomic radius ($R$) is $a = 2\sqrt{2}R$.
    • The number of atoms per unit cell in FCC is $Z_{\text{FCC}} = 6 \times \frac{1}{2} + 8 \times \frac{1}{8} = 4$.
    • The packing fraction (fraction of volume occupied by atoms) is: $$ \text{Packing fraction} = \frac{V_{\text{atoms}}}{V_{\text{cell}}} = \frac{4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3}{(2\sqrt{2}R)^3} \approx 0.74 $$ This value can be used to derive properties like density or atomic radius from experimental measurements.

  • Alpha Particle Kinematics (Example for $E_\alpha \approx 5 \text{ MeV}$):

    • Mass of alpha particle: $m_\alpha \approx 6.645 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$.
    • Velocity of alpha particle: $$ V_\alpha = \sqrt{\frac{2E_\alpha}{m_\alpha}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 5 \times 10^6 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}}{6.645 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}}} \approx 1.53 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s} $$
Lecture 2: August 8, 2023 - Rutherford Scattering Details & Central Potential

Lecture 2: August 8, 2023, Tuesday

  • Scattering Angle Calculation (Simplified):

    • The notes assume a point-like interaction for a classical approach.
    • The change in momentum ($\Delta P$) of an alpha particle due to a collision can be estimated: $$ \Delta P = F \Delta t $$ Where $F$ is the average force and $\Delta t$ is the interaction time. $$ \Delta P = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_p Q_\alpha}{R^2} \frac{2R}{V_\alpha} $$ Here, $R$ is the distance of closest approach (or nuclear radius in a simple model), and $2R/V_\alpha$ is a crude estimate of interaction time.
    • The maximum scattering angle ($\Theta_{\text{max}}$) can be approximated by the ratio of the transverse momentum change to the initial momentum ($P = m_\alpha V_\alpha$): $$ \Theta_{\text{max}} = \frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q_p Q_\alpha}{R^2} \frac{2R}{V_\alpha} \frac{1}{m_\alpha V_\alpha} = \frac{2 Q_p Q_\alpha}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R m_\alpha V_\alpha^2} $$ Using typical values, this yields a very small angle, e.g., $3.3 \times 10^{-4} \text{ rad}$.

  • Gold Foil Details and Scattering Angle:

    • The notes state that a gold foil of $0.4 \text{ }\mu\text{m}$ thickness can contain approximately $2410$ atomic layers.
    • A maximum angle of $46^\circ$ ($0.8 \text{ rad}$) is observed if $\Theta_{\text{max}}$ is the cumulative effect over $2400$ layers.
    • To Increase Scattering Angle: One way to increase the observed scattering angle is to reduce the effective radius of the charge-containing region, implying the charge is concentrated in a tiny volume.

  • Geiger-Marsden-Rutherford (GMR) Investigation Aspects:

    1. Effect of Different Materials: Investigated how the scattering varied with different foil materials while keeping thickness fixed.
    2. Effect of Thickness: Studied the scattering for different thicknesses of the same foil material.
    3. Quantitative Estimation: Performed a quantitative estimation of the number of $\alpha$-particles scattered into a specific solid angle ($d\Omega$). This led to the differential scattering cross-section, $d\sigma/d\Omega$.

  • Experimental Setup and Scintillation:

    • A lead (Pb) tubule was used to collimate the $\alpha$-particle beam and prevent direct hits on the detector by stray particles, ensuring that only particles interacting with the foil reached the detector.
    • The $\alpha$-particles were detected by observing scintillations (light flashes) on a zinc sulfide (ZnS) screen. A microscope was used to count these flashes.
    • The ZnS screen and microscope could be rotated to measure scattered particles at various angles.

  • Scintillation Count vs. Atomic Number (A) / Proton Number (Z):

    • The experimenters measured the ratio of scintillations to the atomic mass ($A$) of the scattering material. The notes state this value was found to be close to the atomic number ($Z$). This indicates that scattering is proportional to $Z^2$, a key feature of Rutherford scattering.

  • Scintillation Count vs. Foil Thickness:

    • For small thicknesses: The number of scintillations increases approximately linearly with thickness. This is because, for very thin foils, the probability of an $\alpha$-particle undergoing a single scattering event increases directly with the number of atoms it encounters.
    • For higher thicknesses: The number of scintillations saturates. This is attributed to:
      • Multiple Scattering: $\alpha$-particles can undergo multiple scattering events within the thicker foil, deviating them from the detector's view or causing them to lose energy.
      • Detector Size: The detector has a fixed size, and beyond a certain thickness, additional scattering events might not direct particles into the detector.
      • Very Large Thicknesses: For extremely large thicknesses, the number of detected particles can actually decrease, as most particles might be absorbed or scattered out of the detection range.

  • Scintillation Count vs. Time:

    • The number of scintillations increases linearly with time, assuming the $\alpha$-source strength (flux) remains constant.

  • Central Potential and Conservation Laws:

    • Definition: A central potential $U(r)$ depends only on the radial distance $r$ from the center of force.
    • Angular Momentum ($\vec{L}$): For a particle moving in a central potential, its angular momentum $\vec{L}$ is conserved (both magnitude and direction).
    • Linear Momentum ($\vec{p}$): Linear momentum $\vec{p}$ is generally not conserved in central potential problems, as the force (directed radially) changes the direction of $\vec{p}$.
    • Energy (E): Total mechanical energy $E = T + U(r)$ is conserved if the central force is conservative.
    • Laplace-Runge-Lenz Vector ($\vec{A}$): For an inverse-square law force (like Coulomb or gravity), the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector $\vec{A} = \vec{p} \times \vec{L} - mk \frac{\vec{r}}{r}$ is also conserved. (Here $k$ is the constant in $F(r) = -k/r^2$).
    • Eccentricity ($e$): For these forces, the eccentricity of the orbit is given by: $$ e^2 = 1 + \frac{2EL^2}{mk^2} $$ Where $m$ is the mass, $E$ is total energy, $L$ is angular momentum, and $k$ is the force constant.
    • The notes state that $\vec{A}$ is not conserved in 4D space but is part of the SO(4) group (symmetry group of 4D rotations related to the bound states of the Kepler problem).
    • Harmonic Oscillator: For a harmonic oscillator, the potential is $V \propto r^2$.

  • Lagrangian and Equations of Motion for Central Potential:

    • Lagrangian $L = T - V = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot{\theta}^2) - V(r)$.
    • The generalized momentum conjugate to $\theta$ is $p_\theta = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}} = mr^2\dot{\theta}$. This is the angular momentum $l$, which is conserved. So, $l = mr^2\dot{\theta}$.
    • The radial equation of motion from Newton's second law ($m\ddot{r} = F_r$) or Euler-Lagrange equation: $$ m\ddot{r} - mr\dot{\theta}^2 = F(r) $$ Where $F(r) = -\frac{\partial V(r)}{\partial r}$. Substituting $\dot{\theta} = l/(mr^2)$: $$ m\ddot{r} - \frac{l^2}{mr^3} = F(r) $$

  • Binet Equation (Converting to Angular Dependence):

    • To remove time dependence and express the radial motion in terms of angle, we use the substitution $u = 1/r$.
    • The Binet equation for central force motion is: $$ \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} + u = -\frac{m}{l^2} \frac{d}{du} V\left(\frac{1}{u}\right) $$
    • This equation is symmetric under $\theta \rightarrow -\theta$ about the turning points.
    • Key Parameters:
      • $r_m$: Distance of closest approach.
      • $b$: Impact parameter.
    • Relation between $b$, $r$, and $\theta$: The geometry implies $b/r = \sin(\pi - \theta)$ (for an incoming trajectory from infinity).
    • Angular Momentum in terms of Impact Parameter: The angular momentum $l$ can also be expressed in terms of the impact parameter $b$ and initial velocity $v_0$: $$ l = |\vec{r} \times m\vec{v}_0| = m v_0 b \sin(\pi - \theta) $$ (The $\sin(\pi-\theta)$ comes from the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{v}_0$ at large distances, it effectively simplifies to $l = m v_0 b$ for an impact parameter from infinity).

  • Binet Equation for Inverse Square Force (Coulomb):

    • For a Coulomb potential, $V(r) = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} u$.
    • Then $\frac{d}{du} V\left(\frac{1}{u}\right) = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}$.
    • Substituting this into the Binet equation: $$ \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} + u = -\frac{m}{l^2} \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} $$
    • Let $k = \frac{m Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 l^2}$. The equation becomes: $$ \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} + u = -k $$ This $k$ here is a constant for a given collision.
Lecture 3: August 10, 2023 - Rutherford Scattering Formula Derivation

Lecture 3: August 10, 2023, Thursday

  • Central Potential Problem (Recap):

    • Potential $V$ depends only on $r$ (radial distance).
    • Angular momentum $\vec{L}$ is conserved (both magnitude and direction) due to rotation symmetry in 3D.
    • Linear momentum $\vec{p}$ is not conserved.

  • Rutherford Scattering Geometry (Cylindrical Symmetry):

    • Consider a cylindrical symmetry about the z-axis, with an incoming beam of $N_0$ alpha particles.
    • The impact parameter is $b$. The scattering takes place off a nucleus.
    • The notes mention "two annular rings have 1:1 mapping among themselves" and "not planar, but they are part of the sphere," referring to how differential cross-section relates impact parameters to solid angles.

  • Differential Scattering Cross-Section ($\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}$):

    • Definition: It's the ratio of the number of particles scattered into a solid angle $d\Omega$ per unit time to the incident intensity (flux).
    • Consider an incident area of an annular ring $dA = 2\pi b db$.
    • The number of particles passing through this ring is $(2\pi b db) I$, where $I$ is the incident intensity.
    • These particles are scattered into a solid angle $d\Omega$. By definition of $d\sigma$: $$ d\sigma = 2\pi b |db| $$ And $d\Omega = 2\pi \sin\theta |d\theta|$.
    • Therefore, the differential cross-section is: $$ \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \frac{2\pi b |db|}{2\pi \sin\theta |d\theta|} = \frac{b}{\sin\theta} \left|\frac{db}{d\theta}\right| $$

  • Solving the Binet Equation for Coulomb Potential (Derivation):

    • The Binet equation for Coulomb potential (from Lecture 2) is: $$ \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} + u = -k $$ where $k = \frac{m Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 l^2}$. (The notes explicitly write this out again).
    • The general solution is: $$ u(\theta) = u_0 \cos(\theta - \theta_m) - k $$ Here, $\theta_m$ defines the turning point and measures the minimum distance from the nucleus. It also represents the angle of symmetry.
    • Boundary Condition 1 (Incoming Particle from Infinity): As $\theta \rightarrow \pi$ (far from interaction, approaching the nucleus from the side where angle is measured from scattering direction), $r \rightarrow \infty$, so $u \rightarrow 0$. $$ 0 = u_0 \cos(\pi - \theta_m) - k $$ $$ \Rightarrow \cos(\pi - \theta_m) = \frac{k}{u_0} $$
    • Boundary Condition 2 (Impact Parameter): From the geometry of scattering, the relation $b/r = \sin(\pi - \theta)$ (or $bu = \sin(\pi - \theta)$) holds. Differentiating with respect to $\theta$: $b \frac{du}{d\theta} = -\cos(\pi - \theta)$.
    • From the solution $u(\theta) = u_0 \cos(\theta - \theta_m) - k$: $$ \frac{du}{d\theta} = -u_0 \sin(\theta - \theta_m) $$
    • At the classical turning point, $\theta = \theta_m$, so $du/d\theta = 0$.
    • The angle of deflection $\Theta$ (scattering angle) is related to $\theta_m$. For incoming particle, angle is measured from initial direction. The general solution implies: $$ \sin(\pi - \theta) = b u_0 \cos(\theta - \theta_m) - b k $$ This line in the notes is an application of the $bu = \sin(\pi-\theta)$ relation, not a derivation.
    • Combining these conditions (specifically for the angle of minimum approach and relation to scattering angle $\Theta$): The total scattering angle $\Theta$ (angle between initial and final velocity vectors) is $\Theta = \pi - 2\theta_m$. This means $\theta_m = (\pi - \Theta)/2$. Using $\cos(\pi - \theta_m) = k/u_0$: $$ \cos\left(\frac{\pi + \Theta}{2}\right) = \frac{k}{u_0} $$ Using trigonometric identity $\cos(\pi/2 + x) = -\sin x$: $$ -\sin\left(\frac{\Theta}{2}\right) = \frac{k}{u_0} $$ Also, from $l = m v_0 b$ and $E = \frac{1}{2}m v_0^2$, we have $l^2 = m^2 v_0^2 b^2 = 2m E b^2$. Recall $k = \frac{m Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 l^2} = \frac{m Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (2m E b^2)} = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E b^2}$. And $u_0 = \frac{1}{r_{min}}$ which relates to the amplitude of the cosine. The standard derivation for $b(\Theta)$ results in: $$ b = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E} \cot\left(\frac{\Theta}{2}\right) $$ (This is equivalent to the notes' expression $b = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 m\hat{v}^2} \cot(\Theta/2)$ if $E = \frac{1}{2}m v^2$).

  • Rutherford Scattering Formula for Differential Cross-Section:

    • Using $b = C \cot(\Theta/2)$, where $C = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E}$.
    • Then $\frac{db}{d\Theta} = -\frac{C}{2} \csc^2(\Theta/2)$.
    • Substitute into the general formula for $d\sigma/d\Omega$: $$ \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \frac{b}{\sin\Theta} \left|\frac{db}{d\Theta}\right| = \frac{C \cot(\Theta/2)}{2\sin(\Theta/2)\cos(\Theta/2)} \frac{C}{2} \csc^2(\Theta/2) $$ $$ = \frac{C^2}{4} \frac{\cos(\Theta/2)}{\sin(\Theta/2)} \frac{1}{\sin(\Theta/2)\cos(\Theta/2)} \frac{1}{\sin^2(\Theta/2)} $$ $$ \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E}\right)^2 \frac{1}{\sin^4(\Theta/2)} $$ The notes show this as: $$ \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 m v^2}\right)^2 \text{cosec}^4\left(\frac{\Theta}{2}\right) $$ This result shows that $\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} \propto \frac{1}{E^2}$.

  • Closest Approach (When $b=0$):

    • For a head-on collision ($b=0$), the alpha particle approaches the nucleus directly. All initial kinetic energy is converted into potential energy at the point of closest approach ($r_m$).
    • By conservation of energy: $E = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_m}$.
    • Therefore, the distance of closest approach is: $$ r_m = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 E} $$ The notes incorrectly state $r_m = b \cot(\Theta/2) / (1-\sin(\Theta/2))$, which is not the standard result for $b=0$.

  • Total Cross-Section ($\sigma_{tot}$):

    • The total cross-section is the integral of the differential cross-section over all solid angles: $$ \sigma_{tot} = \int \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} d\Omega = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E}\right)^2 \frac{1}{\sin^4(\Theta/2)} \sin\Theta d\Theta d\phi $$ Using $\sin\Theta = 2\sin(\Theta/2)\cos(\Theta/2)$ and integrating over $\phi$ gives $2\pi$: $$ \sigma_{tot} = 2\pi \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E}\right)^2 \int_0^\pi \frac{2\sin(\Theta/2)\cos(\Theta/2)}{\sin^4(\Theta/2)} d\Theta $$ $$ = 4\pi \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E}\right)^2 \int_0^\pi \frac{\cos(\Theta/2)}{\sin^3(\Theta/2)} d\Theta $$ Let $x = \cot(\Theta/2)$. Then $dx = -\frac{1}{2}\csc^2(\Theta/2) d\Theta = -\frac{1}{2\sin^2(\Theta/2)} d\Theta$. The integral diverges as $\Theta \rightarrow 0$ (meaning $\sin(\Theta/2) \rightarrow 0$). The notes show: $$ \sigma_{tot} = 8\pi \left(\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 E}\right)^2 \int \frac{d(\sin(\Theta/2))}{\sin^3(\Theta/2)} \rightarrow \infty \quad (\text{at } 0) $$ This divergence is a well-known feature of Rutherford scattering from a point charge; it implies that particles scattered by arbitrarily small angles contribute to the cross-section.
    • Physical Interpretation: In reality, the total cross-section is finite due to screening by atomic electrons or a finite beam size. If there's a minimum scattering angle $\Theta_{min}$, then $\sigma_{tot}$ becomes finite. The notes mention this as "$\sigma_{tot}$ is actually the area." It refers to the effective area for scattering, $ \sigma_{tot} = \pi b_{max}^2 $, where $b_{max}$ corresponds to the minimum detectable scattering angle.

  • Relativistic Scattering of Spin 1/2 Particle (Mott Scattering):

    • For the scattering of electrons (spin 1/2, relativistic) off a point nucleus, the Rutherford formula needs modifications.
    • Mott Scattering Cross-Section: $$ \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{Mott}} = \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{Ruth}} \left(1 - \beta^2 \sin^2\left(\frac{\Theta}{2}\right)\right) $$ where $\beta = V/c$. The term in parentheses accounts for relativistic effects.
    • The notes also include a more complex form for electron scattering: $$ \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_e = \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{Mott}} \frac{E'}{E} \left(1 + 2\tan^2\left(\frac{\Theta}{2}\right)\right) \quad \text{where } \tau = \frac{-e^2}{4\pi\mu e^2} \text{ (likely typo, should be } \tau = \frac{-e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \dots}) $$ The $\frac{E'}{E}$ term is for recoil of the target nucleus.

  • Scattering from an Extended Nucleus (Form Factor):

    • To account for the finite size and charge distribution of the nucleus, a "form factor" $F(q^2)$ is introduced.
    • $$ \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{extended}} = \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{Ruth}} |F(q^2)|^2 $$ Where $q$ is the momentum transfer ($q = |\vec{k_i} - \vec{k_f}|$). The form factor contains all the information about the spatial distribution of charge within the nucleus.
Lecture 4: August 15, 2023 - Electron Scattering & Nuclear Charge Distribution

Lecture 4: August 15, 2023, Tuesday

  • Summary of Rutherford Scattering Dependencies:

    • The differential cross-section ($d\sigma/d\Omega$) is:
      • Proportional to $\text{cosec}^4(\Theta/2)$.
      • Proportional to $1/E^2$ (inverse square of kinetic energy).
      • Proportional to the thickness ($t$) of the target foil.
      • Proportional to the exposure time ($t$). (This should be interpreted as the total count being proportional to time, not the cross-section itself).

  • Determining the Actual Detector Count:

    • Let $\rho$ be the mass density of the target material, $S$ its cross-sectional area, and $t$ its thickness. $A_m$ is the molar mass (or atomic mass).
    • The total number of atoms (nuclei) in the volume $V = St$ is given by: $$ N_{\text{atoms}} = \frac{\rho S t}{A_m} N_A $$ where $N_A$ is Avogadro's number.
    • The earlier derivation of $d\sigma/d\Omega \propto t$ implicitly neglected multiple scatterings. This is valid only for very thin targets.
    • Let $N_0$ be the number of $\alpha$-particles approaching the target.
    • The notes then state "no. of centres available for one $\alpha$-particle" as $N_A \rho t / A_m$. This seems to be the number of nuclei per unit area times the thickness, assuming one scattering center per particle.
    • The total detector count $dN(\Theta)$ for a given scattering angle $\Theta$ is: $$ dN(\Theta) = N_0 \frac{\rho S t N_A}{A_m} \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} $$ (The notes show this as $\frac{dN(\Theta)}{d\Omega} = N_0 \frac{\rho S t N_A}{A_m} \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}$, which is more correct for a differential count).

  • Rutherford Formula Correction and Form Factor (Recap):

    • The Rutherford formula needs correction for finite-sized nuclei. This is done by multiplying by the square of the form factor $|F(q^2)|^2$.
    • $$ \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{extended}} = \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{Ruth}} |F(q^2)|^2 $$ where $q = |\vec{k_i} - \vec{k_f}|$ is the momentum transfer.

  • Electron Scattering for Nuclear Structure:

    • The observed diffraction pattern in electron scattering experiments provides information about the nucleus.
    • Alpha Particles vs. Electrons:
      • For $\alpha$-particles, typical energies are $\approx 5 \text{ MeV}$.
      • The de Broglie wavelength is $\lambda = h/p = \frac{hc}{pc}$.
      • Using $hc \approx 1240 \text{ MeV fm}$, for $5 \text{ MeV}$ $\alpha$-particles, $pc = \sqrt{2m_\alpha c^2 E_k} \approx \sqrt{2 \times 3727 \text{ MeV} \times 5 \text{ MeV}} \approx 193 \text{ MeV}$.
      • So, $\lambda \approx \frac{1240 \text{ MeV fm}}{193 \text{ MeV}} \approx 6.4 \text{ fm}$.
      • The nuclear diameter is typically a few femtometers. Since $\lambda$ is larger than the nuclear size, $\alpha$-particles cannot resolve the internal structure of the nucleus.
      • Therefore, accelerated electrons are used as probes, because their wavelength can be made much smaller (comparable to nuclear size) by increasing their energy.

  • Defining Nuclear Size: Quantum Object:

    • The nucleus is a quantum object and does not have a sharp boundary. Its "size" is defined by its charge distribution.
    • Electron Scattering Selection: To study the nuclear charge distribution, one needs to filter out electrons scattered by the atomic electrons. Electrons that are scattered by the nucleus typically lose very little energy (elastic scattering), whereas those scattered by atomic electrons lose a significant amount of energy. So, selection based on energy loss helps.

  • Wave Functions and Momentum Transfer:

    • Incident and scattered electrons are represented by plane waves (assuming them as free particles far from the nucleus):
      • Incident: $\psi_i(\vec{r}) = e^{i\vec{k}_i \cdot \vec{r}}$
      • Scattered: $\psi_f(\vec{r}) = e^{i\vec{k}_f \cdot \vec{r}}$
    • The change in momentum (momentum transfer) is: $\vec{q} = \vec{k}_i - \vec{k}_f$.
    • The magnitude of momentum transfer is $q = |\vec{k_i} - \vec{k_f}| = 2k \sin(\Theta/2)$ (for elastic scattering where $k_i=k_f=k$).

  • Transition Amplitude and Form Factor (Detailed):

    • The transition amplitude $M_{fi}$ for scattering is given by the matrix element of the potential $V(\vec{r})$ between the initial and final states: $$ M_{fi} = \langle \psi_f | V(\vec{r}) | \psi_i \rangle = \int \psi_f^*(\vec{r}) V(\vec{r}) \psi_i(\vec{r}) d^3r $$ $$ = \int e^{-i\vec{k}_f \cdot \vec{r}} V(\vec{r}) e^{i\vec{k}_i \cdot \vec{r}} d^3r = \int V(\vec{r}) e^{i(\vec{k}_i - \vec{k}_f) \cdot \vec{r}} d^3r = \int V(\vec{r}) e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} d^3r $$
    • The potential $V(\vec{r})$ due to a charge distribution $\rho(\vec{r}')$ is given by Coulomb's law: $$ V(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \int \frac{\rho(\vec{r}')}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|} d^3r' $$
    • Substituting $V(\vec{r})$ into the transition amplitude expression (and assuming electron charge $-e$ interacts with nuclear charge): $$ M_{fi} = -\frac{e}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \int d^3r e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}} \int d^3r' \frac{\rho(\vec{r}')}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|} $$ The integral $\int d^3r \frac{e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}}}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|}$ is the Fourier transform of $1/|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|$, which is $4\pi/q^2 e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}'}$. So, $$ M_{fi} = -\frac{e}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{4\pi}{q^2} \int \rho(\vec{r}') e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}'} d^3r' $$
    • The integral $\int \rho(\vec{r}') e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}'} d^3r'$ is the Fourier Transform of the charge density. For a total nuclear charge $Ze$, the form factor $F(q)$ is defined as: $$ F(q) = \frac{1}{Ze} \int \rho(\vec{r}') e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}'} d^3r' $$ Thus, $M_{fi} \propto F(q)$. The form factor represents the deviation from a point-like nucleus ($F(q)=1$ for a point charge).

  • Charge and Mass Distribution Models:

    • Plots of $\rho(r)$ vs. $r$ are shown for various nuclei (e.g., carbon, helium, gold). These typically show a relatively constant density in the interior and a sharp drop at the surface.
    • Correction Factor for Mass Distribution: To relate the charge distribution (due to protons) to the overall mass distribution (protons + neutrons), the notes assume protons and neutrons are distributed similarly within the nucleus. $$ \rho_{\text{nucleus}} = \rho_{\text{proton}} \times \frac{A}{Z} $$ And similarly: $$ \rho_{\text{neutron}} = \rho_{\text{proton}} \times \frac{A-Z}{Z} $$
Lecture 5: August 17, 2023 - Form Factor & Nuclear Radius

Lecture 5: August 17, 2023, Thursday

  • Recap: Form Factor and Electron Scattering:

    • The extended scattering cross-section: $$ \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{extended}} = \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_{\text{Ruth}} |F(q^2)|^2 $$
    • The form factor $F(q)$ is the Fourier Transform (F.T.) of the charge density $\rho(\vec{r})$.
    • Electron scattering is used (instead of alpha particles) because electrons can have high energies and therefore small wavelengths, allowing them to probe the internal structure of the nucleus.
    • The explicit form of the form factor (including $\hbar$ for clarity, usually omitted in natural units): $$ F(q) = \frac{1}{Ze} \int e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}/\hbar} \rho(\vec{r}) d^3r $$
    • Normalization Condition: For a point nucleus, $F(0) = 1$. This means the integral of the charge density is the total charge $Ze$.
    • The extended cross-section becomes equal to the Rutherford cross-section when $F(q) = 1$ (effectively when $q \rightarrow 0$, or when the wavelength is much larger than the nucleus, making it appear point-like).

  • Inverse Fourier Transform to Obtain Charge Density:

    • Since $F(q)$ is the F.T. of $\rho(r)$, $\rho(r)$ can be obtained by the inverse F.T. of $F(q)$: $$ \rho(\vec{r}) = \frac{Ze}{(2\pi\hbar)^3} \int F(q) e^{-i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}/\hbar} d^3q $$ (The notes show $d^3q'$ and $F(q')$ but it should be $d^3q$ and $F(q)$ for consistency).

  • Special Case: Spherical Charge Distribution:

    • For a spherically symmetric charge distribution $\rho(r)$, the form factor simplifies (after integrating over angular parts of $d^3r$): $$ F(q) = \frac{4\pi}{Ze q} \int_0^\infty r \rho(r) \sin\left(\frac{qr}{\hbar}\right) dr $$
    • Example: Uniform Spherical Distribution:
      • $\rho(r) = \frac{3Ze}{4\pi R^3}$ for $r \le R$
      • $\rho(r) = 0$ for $r > R$
      For this distribution, the form factor is: $$ F(q) = \frac{3}{(qR/\hbar)^3} \left\{\sin\left(\frac{qR}{\hbar}\right) - \left(\frac{qR}{\hbar}\right)\cos\left(\frac{qR}{\hbar}\right)\right\} $$

  • Mean Square Charge Radius ($\langle r^2 \rangle$):

    • The mean square charge radius is defined as: $$ \langle r^2 \rangle = \frac{1}{Ze} \int r^2 \rho(r) d^3r $$
    • To relate this to the form factor, expand $e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}/\hbar}$ in the form factor integral using Taylor series: $$ e^{i\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}/\hbar} = 1 + i\frac{\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}}{\hbar} - \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}}{\hbar}\right)^2 + \dots $$
    • For spherical symmetry, $\int \rho(r) d^3r = Ze$. The term $\int i\frac{\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r}}{\hbar} \rho(r) d^3r = 0$ (due to symmetry).
    • The third term becomes $-\frac{1}{2\hbar^2} \int (\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r})^2 \rho(r) d^3r$. For spherical symmetry, $\langle (\vec{q} \cdot \vec{r})^2 \rangle = \langle q^2 r^2 \cos^2\theta \rangle = \frac{1}{3} q^2 \langle r^2 \rangle$.
    • So, expanding $F(q)$ for small $q$: $$ F(q) \approx \frac{1}{Ze} \left[ Ze - \frac{1}{2\hbar^2} \frac{q^2}{3} \int r^2 \rho(r) d^3r \right] = 1 - \frac{q^2}{6\hbar^2} \langle r^2 \rangle $$
    • Therefore, the mean square charge radius can be obtained from the derivative of the form factor at $q=0$: $$ \langle r^2 \rangle = -6\hbar^2 \left.\frac{dF(q^2)}{dq^2}\right|_{q^2=0} $$

  • Mass Distribution and Woods-Saxon Fit:

    • The notes assume that neutrons are arranged in a similar way to protons, leading to a similar density profile for both.
    • Woods-Saxon Potential/Density Profile: A common empirical fit for the nuclear matter density $\rho(r)$ is the Woods-Saxon form: $$ \rho(r) = \frac{\rho_0}{1 + e^{(r-R)/a}} $$
      • $\rho_0$: Central density.
      • $R$: Half-density radius (where density is $\rho_0/2$).
      • $a$: Diffuseness parameter, related to the surface thickness.
    • Surface Thickness: The distance between the 90% and 10% density points is approximately $4.4a$.
      • By setting $\rho(r)/\rho_0 = 0.9$ and $0.1$, solving for $r_1$ and $r_2$, and taking the difference: $r_1 \approx R - 2.2a$ and $r_2 \approx R + 2.2a$.
      • So, $r_2 - r_1 = 4.4a$.
      From experiments, $a \approx 0.5 \text{ fm}$, leading to a surface thickness of $2.2 \text{ fm}$, which is approximately constant for all nuclei.
    • Nuclear Radius ($R$): Since $\rho_0$ is approximately constant, the nuclear volume is proportional to the total number of nucleons (mass number $A$).
      • $V \propto A \implies R^3 \propto A \implies R = R_0 A^{1/3}$.
      • From experimental fits, $R_0 \approx 1.2 \text{ fm}$, which is also approximately constant for all nuclei.

  • Nuclear Force:

    • The nucleus is a bound object due to the attractive nuclear force (strong force).
    • Unlike gravity, which is a long-range force, the nuclear force is a short-range force.

  • Stability of Nuclei:

    1. Stable Nuclei: Nuclei that do not spontaneously decay under natural conditions. There are 80 elements with 252 stable isotopes.
    2. Radioactive Nuclei: Nuclei that undergo spontaneous decay.
      • Primordial Isotopes: Long-lived isotopes that have existed since the formation of the Earth (e.g., $^{238}\text{U}$ with $T_{1/2} \approx 4.5$ billion years, $^{235}\text{U}$ with $T_{1/2} \approx 700$ million years). There are 34 known primordial isotopes.
      • Non-Primordial Isotopes: Shorter-lived radioactive isotopes created naturally (e.g., cosmic ray interactions) or artificially. 53 such isotopes have been observed.
    • N vs. Z Plot (Chart of Nuclides): A plot of neutron number (N) versus proton number (Z) shows a "band of stability."
      • For light nuclei, N $\approx$ Z for stability.
      • For heavy nuclei, N $>$ Z for stability, due to increasing Coulomb repulsion between protons.
    • Nuclear Reactions Example (Fission): $$ ^{235}_{92}\text{U} + n \rightarrow ^{144}_{56}\text{Ba} + ^{89}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3n $$ This reaction produces more neutrons, which can induce further fissions, leading to a chain reaction.
Lecture 6: August 19, 2023 - K-Alpha X-ray Method & Muonic Atoms

Lecture 6: August 19, 2023, Saturday

  • How to Determine Charge Distribution?

    1. Electron scattering (discussed in previous lectures).
    2. K-alpha (K-$\alpha$) X-ray method.

  • K-alpha X-ray Method to Determine $\rho(r)$:

    • Electron Orbit: In an atom, an electron's orbit is not fixed; its wave function is spread out.
    • Ground State Wave Function (H-atom): For the 1s ground state of a hydrogenic atom, the wave function is: $$ \psi_{1s}(r) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \left(\frac{Z}{a_0}\right)^{3/2} e^{-Zr/a_0} $$ where $a_0$ is the Bohr radius.
    • Probability Inside Nucleus: At $r=0$, $\psi_{1s}(0)$ is non-zero. This means there is a non-zero probability of finding the electron inside the nucleus.
    • Energy Shift Due to Finite Nuclear Size: If the electron is part of an extended nucleus (not a point charge), the ground state energy is no longer simply $-13.6 \text{ eV}$ (the value derived assuming a point nucleus).
    • Potential for Extended Nucleus: The electrostatic potential $V(r)$ due to a uniformly charged sphere of radius $R$ and total charge $Ze$ is:
      • For $r \le R$: $V(r) = -\frac{Ze}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R} \left(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{r^2}{2R^2}\right)$
      • For $r > R$: $V(r) = -\frac{Ze}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}$

  • Perturbation Theory Application:

    • The actual Hamiltonian $H$ can be written as $H = H_0 + \Delta H$, where $H_0$ is the Hamiltonian for a point nucleus and $\Delta H$ is the perturbation due to the finite size of the nucleus.
    • The perturbation potential $\Delta V$ is the difference between the potential of an extended nucleus and a point nucleus: $$ \Delta V = V_{\text{extended}}(r) - V_{\text{point}}(r) $$ $$ \Delta V = -\frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R} \left(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{r^2}{2R^2}\right) + \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} \quad \text{for } r \le R $$ $$ \Delta V = 0 \quad \text{for } r > R $$
    • The change in the ground state energy ($\Delta E$) due to this perturbation, to first order, is $\Delta E = \langle \psi_{1s} | \Delta V | \psi_{1s} \rangle$.
    • For the 1s state, assuming $r \ll a_0$ (which is true for heavy nuclei), $\psi_{1s}(r)$ can be approximated by $\psi_{1s}(0)$.
    • The integral for $\Delta E$ is: $$ \Delta E = \int_0^R |\psi_{1s}(r)|^2 \left[ -\frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R} \left(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{r^2}{2R^2}\right) + \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} \right] 4\pi r^2 dr $$ $$ \Delta E = \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} |\psi_{1s}(0)|^2 \int_0^R \left[ -\frac{3}{2R} + \frac{r^2}{2R^3} + \frac{1}{r} \right] 4\pi r^2 dr $$ $$ \Delta E = \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Z^3}{\pi a_0^3} \int_0^R \left( -\frac{3r^2}{2R} + \frac{r^4}{2R^3} + r \right) dr $$ $$ \Delta E = \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Z^3}{\pi a_0^3} \left[ -\frac{r^3}{2R} + \frac{r^5}{10R^3} + \frac{r^2}{2} \right]_0^R $$ $$ \Delta E = \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Z^3}{\pi a_0^3} \left[ -\frac{R^2}{2} + \frac{R^2}{10} + \frac{R^2}{2} \right] = \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Z^3}{\pi a_0^3} \frac{R^2}{10} $$ $$ \Delta E = \frac{Z^4 e^2 R^2}{40\pi\epsilon_0 a_0^3} $$ This shows $\Delta E \propto 1/a_0^3 \propto m^3$ (since $a_0 \propto 1/m$).

  • Experimental Measurement (Isotope Shifts):

    • The energy shift is very small, so it's difficult to measure directly. Instead, the difference in K-alpha X-ray energies between two isotopes of the same element ($A$ and $A'$) is measured.
    • The nuclear radius $R$ is given by $R = R_0 A^{1/3}$.
    • The energy difference $\Delta E_{\text{K}\alpha} = \Delta E_{1s}(A) - \Delta E_{1s}(A')$. $$ \Delta E_{\text{K}\alpha} = \frac{Z^4 e^2 R_0^2}{40\pi\epsilon_0 a_0^3} (A^{2/3} - A'^{2/3}) $$ The notes have the factor of $2/5$ and a negative sign for the difference in K-alpha energies. The specific form might depend on which isotope is subtracted from which. $$ \Delta E = -\frac{2}{5} \frac{Z e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\langle r^2 \rangle}{a_0^3} Z^2 $$ Using $\langle r^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{5}R^2$ for uniform charge distribution, this matches.
    • Experimental plots for mercury (Hg) isotopes show this linear dependence on $A^{2/3}$, with about $10\%$ accuracy compared to theoretical predictions.

  • Muonic Hydrogen Problem:

    • Muons ($\mu$) are leptons, similar to electrons, but much heavier ($m_\mu \approx 207 m_e$).
    • In a muonic atom, a muon orbits the nucleus instead of an electron.
    • Since the Bohr radius $a_0 \propto 1/m$, a muon's orbit is much closer to the nucleus ($a_\mu \approx a_0/207$).
    • This makes muonic X-rays (transitions) much more sensitive to the finite size of the nucleus, as the muon spends more time inside or very close to the nucleus. This allows for more precise measurements of nuclear charge radii.
Lecture 7: August 22, 2023 - Nuclear Stability & Binding Energy

Lecture 7: August 22, 2023, Tuesday

  • Stability of Nuclei (Factors Affecting):

    • Proton-Proton Repulsion: Protons are positively charged and repel each other via Coulomb force. A nucleus made of only 5 protons cannot form a bound state.
    • Neutron-Neutron Stability: Similarly, 5 neutrons alone cannot form a stable configuration in a nucleus. (Free neutrons decay).
    • Neutron Stars: However, a very large number of neutrons ($\sim 10^{50}$) can form a stable neutron star due to strong gravitational forces.

  • N vs. Z Stability Line:

    • A plot of neutron number (N) vs. proton number (Z) shows the "valley of stability."
    • Lighter Nuclei: For lighter nuclei, stability is favored when $N \approx Z$.
    • Heavier Nuclei: For heavier nuclei, stability requires $N > Z$ because the increasing Coulomb repulsion between protons necessitates a larger number of neutrons (which provide additional attractive strong force without adding Coulomb repulsion) to maintain binding.

  • Beta Decay and Proton/Neutron Rich Nuclei:

    • Proton-Rich Nuclei (Too many protons relative to neutrons): Undergo $\beta^+$ decay (positron emission) or electron capture.
      • $\beta^+$ decay: $p \rightarrow n + e^+ + \nu_e$.
        • For a free proton, this decay is forbidden as $m_p < m_n + m_e$.
        • However, it is possible inside the nucleus if the binding energy of the daughter nucleus compensates for the mass difference.
        • Example energy conversion: If $m_p c^2 = 938.27 \text{ MeV}$, $m_n c^2 = 939.57 \text{ MeV}$, $m_e c^2 = 0.511 \text{ MeV}$.
        • For $p \rightarrow n + e^+ + \nu_e$, mass difference is $(m_n + m_{e^+} + m_{\nu_e}) - m_p \approx (939.57 + 0.511 + 0) - 938.27 = 1.811 \text{ MeV}$. This means energy is required, so a free proton cannot decay this way.
        • This highlights that energy is not conserved for a free proton decay. It's only allowed within the nucleus due to the change in nuclear binding energy.
    • Neutron-Rich Nuclei (Too many neutrons relative to protons): Undergo $\beta^-$ decay (electron emission).
      • $\beta^-$ decay: $n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$.
        • For a free neutron, this decay is allowed as $m_n > m_p + m_e$.
        • Mass difference: $m_n - (m_p + m_{e^-} + m_{\bar{\nu}_e}) \approx 939.57 - (938.27 + 0.511 + 0) = 0.789 \text{ MeV}$. This is the Q-value, which is positive, so it's allowed.
    • The notes state for GUT (Grand Unified Theory) to be successful, $p^+$ decay should happen freely, but this would violate baryon number conservation and is not observed.
    • Neutron-rich nuclei can achieve stability by directly emitting a neutron, rather than $\beta^-$ decay, if energetically favorable.

  • Binding Energy (BE) of Nuclei:

    • Definition: The binding energy of a nucleus is the energy required to separate it into its constituent protons and neutrons. A higher binding energy per nucleon generally implies greater nuclear stability.
    • Mass Defect Relation: The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of the masses of its individual nucleons. This "mass defect" corresponds to the binding energy. $$ M(Z, A)c^2 = Z m_p c^2 + (A-Z) m_n c^2 - \Omega_{BE}(X) $$ Where $M(Z,A)$ is the nuclear mass, $m_p$ and $m_n$ are proton and neutron masses, $Z$ is proton number, $A$ is mass number. $\Omega_{BE}(X)$ is the nuclear binding energy.
    • Atomic Mass vs. Nuclear Mass:
      • It's often more convenient to work with atomic masses due to experimental measurements. An atomic mass $M_A(Z,A)$ includes the mass of $Z$ electrons.
      • $M_A(Z,A)c^2 = Z m_H c^2 + (A-Z) m_n c^2 - \Omega_{BE}(X)$
      • Here, $m_H c^2 = (m_p + m_e) c^2 - BE_e(H)$, where $BE_e(H)$ is the atomic binding energy of hydrogen, which is negligible compared to nuclear binding energies. So, $m_H \approx m_p + m_e$.
      • The binding energy of an $\alpha$-particle ($^4\text{He}$) is approximately $28 \text{ MeV}$.
    • Estimating $\Omega_{BE}(X)$: This quantity is difficult to estimate directly but is of the same order as $Z \times BE_e(H)$. The difference between atomic and nuclear binding energies can often be neglected for rough calculations.
    • Binding Energy Per Nucleon ($\Omega_{BE}/A$):
      • When plotted against mass number $A$, this curve rises sharply for light nuclei, peaks around $A=50-60$ (iron/nickel region, $\approx 8 \text{ MeV/nucleon}$), and then slowly decreases for heavier nuclei.
      • The fact that BE/A is relatively constant for most nuclei (around 8 MeV) suggests a saturation property of the nuclear force.

  • Semi-Empirical Mass Formula (SEMF):

    • This formula provides an approximate theoretical description of nuclear binding energy based on classical liquid drop model and quantum mechanical considerations.
    • It consists of several terms contributing to the total binding energy ($\Omega_{BE}$):
      1. Volume Term ($a_v A$):
        • Initial Assumption: If every nucleon interacted equally with every other nucleon, there would be $A(A-1)/2$ pairs. So, total BE $\propto A^2$.
        • Correction: However, the nuclear force is short-ranged and saturates. Each nucleon only interacts with its immediate neighbors. So, the contribution is proportional to the number of nucleons ($A$).
        • Contribution: $+a_v A$. ($a_v \approx 15.8 \text{ MeV}$).
      2. Surface Term ($-a_s A^{2/3}$):
        • Correction to Volume Term: Nucleons on the surface of the nucleus have fewer neighbors than those in the interior. This reduces their binding.
        • The number of surface nucleons is proportional to the nuclear surface area, which is $4\pi R^2 = 4\pi (R_0 A^{1/3})^2 \propto A^{2/3}$.
        • Contribution: $-a_s A^{2/3}$. ($a_s \approx 18.3 \text{ MeV}$). This term reduces the binding energy.
      3. Coulomb Term ($-a_c Z(Z-1)/A^{1/3}$):
        • Correction: Protons are positively charged and repel each other. This electrostatic repulsion reduces the binding energy.
        • The electrostatic potential energy of a uniformly charged sphere of charge $Ze$ and radius $R$ is $E_C = \frac{3}{5} \frac{(Ze)^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R}$.
        • For discrete charges, it's more accurate to use $Z(Z-1)$ instead of $Z^2$.
        • Using $R = R_0 A^{1/3}$: $$ E_C = \frac{3}{5} \frac{Z(Z-1)e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R_0 A^{1/3}} $$
        • Contribution: $-a_c \frac{Z(Z-1)}{A^{1/3}}$. ($a_c \approx 0.714 \text{ MeV}$). This term also reduces binding energy.
Lecture 8: August 24, 2023 - Semi-Empirical Mass Formula (Cont.)

Lecture 8: August 24, 2023, Thursday

  • Semi-Empirical Mass Formula (SEMF) Continued:

    • The total binding energy $\Omega_{BE}$ is related to the atomic mass $M(Z,A)$ by: $$ M(Z,A)c^2 = Zm_p c^2 + (A-Z)m_n c^2 - \Omega_{BE} $$ (Using proton and neutron masses, not atomic hydrogen mass here for nuclear binding energy).

  • Other Terms (Quantum Mechanical Origin):

    1. Asymmetry Term ($-a_a \frac{(A-2Z)^2}{A}$):
      • Reason: Quantum mechanically, nucleons are fermions and obey the Pauli Exclusion Principle. They fill energy levels in the nuclear potential well. For a stable nucleus, it is energetically favorable to have roughly equal numbers of protons and neutrons (i.e., $N \approx Z$), especially for lighter nuclei.
      • If $N \ne Z$, the excess nucleons (protons or neutrons) must occupy higher energy levels, leading to a reduction in the overall binding energy.
      • The term $(A-2Z)^2 = (N-Z)^2$, so it penalizes deviations from $N=Z$. The $1/A$ factor reflects that the energy penalty per nucleon decreases for larger nuclei, as there are more available energy levels.
      • Contribution: $-a_a \frac{(A-2Z)^2}{A}$. ($a_a \approx 23.2 \text{ MeV}$). This term reduces binding energy.
      • Explanation from Fermi Gas Model: Imagine two potential wells, one for protons and one for neutrons. To minimize total energy, the Fermi levels for protons and neutrons should be equal. If $N \ne Z$, the Fermi level for the more numerous type of nucleon is higher, leading to an energy penalty proportional to $(N-Z)^2/A$.
    2. Pairing Term ($\pm a_p A^{-k}$):
      • Reason: Nucleons tend to pair up in energy levels (two protons with opposite spins, or two neutrons with opposite spins). Paired nucleons are more strongly bound.
      • Contribution:
        • $+a_p A^{-k}$ for Even-Even (EE) nuclei (even Z, even N). These are the most stable.
        • $0$ for Even-Odd (EO) or Odd-Even (OE) nuclei (one unpaired nucleon).
        • $-a_p A^{-k}$ for Odd-Odd (OO) nuclei (both Z and N are odd). These are the least stable.
      • The exponent $k$ is usually $1/2$ or $3/4$. ($a_p \approx 12 \text{ MeV}$ for $k=1/2$, or $34 \text{ MeV}$ for $k=3/4$).

  • Complete Semi-Empirical Mass Formula:

    $$ \Omega_{BE}(A,Z) = a_v A - a_s A^{2/3} - a_c \frac{Z(Z-1)}{A^{1/3}} - a_a \frac{(A-2Z)^2}{A} \pm a_p A^{-k} $$

  • Fitting to Experimental Data:

    • The values of the coefficients ($a_v, a_s, a_c, a_a, a_p$) are determined by fitting this formula to experimental atomic mass data using a least-squares method (minimizing $\chi^2$).
    • Typical fitted values (from the notes):
      • $a_v \approx 15.8 \text{ MeV}$
      • $a_s \approx 18.3 \text{ MeV}$
      • $a_c \approx 0.711 \text{ MeV}$ (sometimes 0.69 MeV from theoretical constants)
      • $a_a \approx 23.2 \text{ MeV}$
      • $a_p \approx 12 \text{ MeV}$ (for $k=-1/2$) or $34 \text{ MeV}$ (for $k=-3/4$)

  • Surface Term Derivation (Example from notes' margin):

    • Imagine nucleons in a cubic volume. The number of nucleons on the surface is proportional to $A^{2/3}$.
    • Volume occupied by surface nucleons = $4\pi R^2 t$ where $t$ is surface thickness.
    • Number of surface nucleons = $4\pi R^2 t / (\text{volume of 1 nucleon}) \approx 4\pi (R_0 A^{1/3})^2 (a) / (\frac{4}{3}\pi r_0^3)$
    • This leads to the factor $4.8 A^{2/3}$ for the number of surface nucleons, which justifies the $A^{2/3}$ dependence of the surface term.
Lecture 9: August 31, 2023 - Separation Energy & Isobaric Parabolas

Lecture 9: August 31, 2023, Thursday

  • Estimating the BE/A Curve from SEMF:

    • Plotting each term's contribution to BE/A shows how the overall curve of BE/A vs. A is shaped.
    • Volume term ($a_v$): Constant, contributing about $15.8 \text{ MeV}$/nucleon.
    • Volume + Surface ($a_v - a_s/A^{1/3}$): The surface term reduces BE/A, especially for light nuclei, as a larger fraction of nucleons are on the surface.
    • Volume + Surface + Coulomb ($a_v - a_s/A^{1/3} - a_c Z(Z-1)/(A^{4/3})$): Coulomb repulsion further reduces BE/A, particularly for heavy nuclei (larger Z).
    • Full SEMF: Adding the asymmetry and pairing terms results in a curve that accurately matches experimental data, peaking around $A=56$ (Iron) and then decreasing for heavier nuclei.

  • Separation Energy:

    • Neutron Separation Energy ($S_n$): The energy required to remove one neutron from a nucleus $(Z,N)$. $$ S_n = BE(Z, N) - BE(Z, N-1) $$ This value can be positive or negative depending on the stability.
    • Plot of $S_n$ vs. Number of Neutrons Removed:
      • Shows an overall decreasing trend (it becomes harder to remove neutrons as the nucleus becomes proton-rich).
      • Exhibits a "sawtooth" behavior: $S_n$ is higher when removing an even neutron (from a paired state) and lower when removing an odd neutron (breaking a pair or removing an unpaired one). This is a direct consequence of the pairing term in the SEMF.
    • Proton Separation Energy ($S_p$): Similar behavior is observed for removing protons. $$ S_p = BE(Z, N) - BE(Z-1, N) $$

  • Isobaric Parabola (Fixed $A$):

    • For a fixed mass number $A$, the atomic mass $M(Z, A)$ can be expressed as a quadratic function of $Z$: $$ M(Z, A) = \alpha Z^2 + \beta Z + \gamma $$
      • Here, $\alpha = \frac{m_n - m_p}{2A} + \frac{a_c}{A^{1/3}} + \frac{4a_a}{A}$. (Corrected to $m_n - m_p$ which appears in the notes).
      • $\beta = m_p - m_n - \frac{a_c}{A^{1/3}} - \frac{4a_a}{A}$ (This is typically $m_p$ for the linear Z term).
      • $\gamma = m_n A - a_v A + a_s A^{2/3} + a_a A \pm a_p A^{-k}$. (Constants terms).
    • Most Stable Nucleus for a Given $A$: The minimum of this parabola corresponds to the most stable nucleus ($Z_{min}$) for a given $A$. It is found by setting $\frac{dM(Z,A)}{dZ} = 0$: $$ 2\alpha Z_{min} + \beta = 0 \implies Z_{min} = -\frac{\beta}{2\alpha} $$
    • Example for $A=125$: From experimental data, $Z_{min} \approx 52.1$ (which corresponds to Tellurium, $^{125}\text{Te}$).
    • Parabolas for Even $A$:
      • For Even $A$ nuclei, the pairing term causes two distinct parabolas: one for Even-Even (EE) nuclei (lower mass, more stable) and one for Odd-Odd (OO) nuclei (higher mass, less stable).
      • This explains why nuclei like $^{128}\text{I}$ might not be absolutely stable and can undergo $\beta^+$ or $\beta^-$ decay to reach the lower EE parabola.
    • General Formula for $Z_{min}$: $$ Z_{min} = \frac{A/2}{1 + \frac{a_c}{4a_a} A^{2/3}} $$ This formula shows that for heavier nuclei, $Z_{min}$ is less than $A/2$, consistent with the $N > Z$ trend for stability.

  • Double Beta Decay:

    • This occurs when a nucleus transitions from an Odd-Odd state to a more stable Even-Even state by emitting two beta particles simultaneously, bypassing an energetically unfavorable intermediate Odd-Odd state.
    • Example: $^{52}\text{Te} \rightarrow ^{52}\text{Xe} + 2e^- + 2\bar{\nu}_e$.
    • Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay ($2\nu\text{-less } \beta\beta$): A hypothetical process where no neutrinos are emitted (e.g., $2n \rightarrow 2p + 2e^-$). Its observation would imply that neutrinos are Majorana particles (their own antiparticles).
    • Neutrino vs. Anti-neutrino: How to differentiate? Neutrinos are uncharged and have very small magnetic moments (if any). If neutrinos were Majorana particles, there would be no distinction between neutrino and anti-neutrino.
Lecture 10: September 5, 2023 - Fermi Gas Model of Nucleus

Lecture 10: September 5, 2023, Tuesday

  • Fermi Gas Model of Nucleus:

    • A simple, primitive model where nucleons are treated as a degenerate Fermi gas confined within a potential well, similar to electrons in a metal.
    • Assumes particles are "quasi-free" (meaning their binding energy is much less than their kinetic energy, $BE \ll KE$). This is not strictly true for nuclei, but it provides a starting point.
    • Potential Well: Nucleons are in a square potential well. For neutrons, the well is approximately square. For protons, the well has a Coulomb potential "bump" that falls off as $1/r$ outside the nucleus.

  • Fermi Level and Filling of States:

    • Nucleons are fermions, so they obey the Pauli Exclusion Principle and fill available energy states from the lowest energy level upwards without gaps.

  • 3D Quantum Well (Particle in a Box):

    • Energy levels are given by: $$ E_{n_x, n_y, n_z} = \frac{\pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2} (n_x^2 + n_y^2 + n_z^2) $$ where $n_x, n_y, n_z = 1, 2, 3, \dots$. (For $H$-atom, $n=0$ is possible for $S$-waves, but usually $n \ge 1$ for box).
    • Momentum Space: For a large system, the states can be viewed in momentum space. The number of states with momentum up to $p_F$ is approximately the volume of a sphere in momentum space.
    • Number of States ($N$):
      • Each state can hold two particles (spin up/down).
      • The volume in momentum space for one particle (per unit volume) is $(2\pi\hbar)^3$.
      • So, $N = 2 \times \frac{1}{8} \times \frac{4}{3}\pi p_F^3 \times \frac{V}{(2\pi\hbar)^3} = \frac{1}{3\pi^2} \left(\frac{p_F}{\hbar}\right)^3 V$. (This is the standard form, the notes have $\frac{1}{3}\pi (\frac{P_F L}{\pi\hbar})^3$, possibly an intermediate step or slight variation).
    • So, the number density is $n = N/V = \frac{1}{3\pi^2} (p_F/\hbar)^3$.
    • Fermi Momentum ($p_F$): $$ p_F = \hbar (3\pi^2 n)^{1/3} $$
    • Application to Nucleus:
      • Volume of nucleus $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi (R_0 A^{1/3})^3$.
      • For neutrons, $n_n = N/V$. For protons, $n_p = Z/V$.
      • Assuming $N \approx Z \approx A/2$, then $n = A/V = \frac{A}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R_0^3 A} = \frac{3}{4\pi R_0^3}$.
      • So, $p_F = \hbar \left(3\pi^2 \frac{3}{4\pi R_0^3}\right)^{1/3} = \hbar \left(\frac{9\pi}{4R_0^3}\right)^{1/3} = \frac{\hbar}{R_0} \left(\frac{9\pi}{4}\right)^{1/3}$.
      • Using $R_0 \approx 1.2 \text{ fm}$, $p_F \approx 250 \text{ MeV/c}$.

  • Fermi Energy ($E_F$):

    • $E_F = \frac{p_F^2}{2m}$.
    • For nucleons, $E_F \approx \frac{(250 \text{ MeV/c})^2}{2 \times 939 \text{ MeV/c}^2} \approx 33 \text{ MeV}$. This represents the maximum kinetic energy of a nucleon at 0 K.
    • Depth of Potential Well ($V_0$):
      • The depth of the potential well is approximately the Fermi energy plus the average binding energy (from SEMF).
      • $V_0 = E_F + BE/A \approx 33 \text{ MeV} + 8 \text{ MeV} = 41 \text{ MeV}$.
      • The notes comment this model gives "sensible numbers."

  • Number Density in Momentum Space:

    • $dn = 4\pi p^2 dp \times (2 \times \frac{1}{8}) \frac{V}{(2\pi\hbar)^3} = \frac{V}{2\pi^2\hbar^3} p^2 dp$.
    • Average Momentum ($\langle p \rangle$): $$ \langle p \rangle = \frac{\int_0^{p_F} p dn}{\int_0^{p_F} dn} = \frac{\int_0^{p_F} p^3 dp}{\int_0^{p_F} p^2 dp} = \frac{p_F^4/4}{p_F^3/3} = \frac{3}{4} p_F $$ So, $\langle p \rangle \approx \frac{3}{4} \times 250 \text{ MeV/c} \approx 190 \text{ MeV/c}$.
    • Average Energy ($\langle E \rangle$): $$ \langle E \rangle = \frac{\int_0^{p_F} E dn}{\int_0^{p_F} dn} = \frac{\int_0^{p_F} (p^2/2m) p^2 dp}{\int_0^{p_F} p^2 dp} = \frac{(1/2m) p_F^5/5}{p_F^3/3} = \frac{3}{5} \frac{p_F^2}{2m} = \frac{3}{5} E_F $$ So, $\langle E \rangle \approx \frac{3}{5} \times 33 \text{ MeV} \approx 20 \text{ MeV}$.

  • Total Kinetic Energy ($E_{KE}$):

    • $E_{KE} = N \langle E_n \rangle + Z \langle E_p \rangle$.
    • For ground state (assuming $N=Z=A/2$ and equal $\langle E \rangle$ for p and n): $$ E_{KE} = (N+Z) \langle E \rangle = A \langle E \rangle \approx 20 A \text{ MeV} $$ The total energy of the nucleus $E_{total} = E_{KE} - \Omega_{BE}$.
    • The notes link the kinetic energy calculation to the volume term of the SEMF ($a_v A \approx 15.8 A$). The Fermi gas model overestimates this term.

  • Asymmetry Term from Fermi Gas Model:

    • To derive the asymmetry term, consider the total kinetic energy when $N \ne Z$. $$ E_{KE} = \frac{3}{10m} \left(\frac{9\pi}{4}\right)^{2/3} \frac{\hbar^2}{R_0^2} \frac{1}{A^{2/3}} \left\{N^{5/3} + Z^{5/3}\right\} $$
    • Substitute $N = A/2 + (N-Z)/2$ and $Z = A/2 - (N-Z)/2$. Expand using $(1+x)^{5/3} \approx 1 + \frac{5}{3}x + \frac{5}{9}x^2$.
    • The second term in the expansion leads to the asymmetry term proportional to $(N-Z)^2/A$.
    • The coefficient $a_a$ derived from the Fermi gas model is approximately $11 \text{ MeV}$.
    • Comparison: The fitted value from experiments is $a_a \approx 23 \text{ MeV}$.
    • Conclusion: The Fermi gas model "utterly fails" to accurately predict the asymmetry term because it neglects the strong interactions between protons and neutrons, which are spin-dependent and short-range.

  • Degenerate Fermi Gas for White Dwarfs:

    • The concept of a degenerate Fermi gas also applies to white dwarf stars, where electron degeneracy pressure provides stability against gravitational collapse.
Lecture 11: September 7, 2023 - White Dwarfs & Neutron Stars

Lecture 11: September 7, 2023, Thursday

  • White Dwarfs:

    • Composed of intact atoms.
    • Experience Van der Waals interactions (residual electromagnetic force between neutral atoms). This is an "artifact of Coulomb interaction" and not fundamental at the stellar scale.
    • Stability is maintained by a balance between Fermi degeneracy pressure (primarily from electrons) and thermal pressure against gravitational forces.

  • Formation of Neutron Stars:

    • If the gravitational pull in a white dwarf exceeds a critical limit, the electron degeneracy pressure can no longer support it.
    • Electrons are captured by protons (inverse beta decay: $p + e^- \rightarrow n + \nu_e$), converting most of the matter into neutrons.
    • This process leads to the creation of a neutron star.

  • Chandrasekhar Limit:

    • The maximum mass a white dwarf can support is called the Chandrasekhar Limit ($M_{Ch} \approx 1.4 \text{ M}_\odot$, where $\text{M}_\odot$ is solar mass). Beyond this, it collapses.
    • Neutron stars are also supported by Fermi degeneracy pressure (of neutrons).
    • A neutron star has a maximum stable mass, sometimes referred to as the "second Chandrasekhar limit" or Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) limit, typically around $2.2 \text{ M}_\odot$. Beyond this, it may collapse into a quark star or black hole.

  • Magnetic Fields of Compact Stars:

    • Magnetic flux ($\Phi = B \cdot A$, where $B$ is magnetic field and $A$ is area) is approximately conserved during stellar collapse.
    • When a white dwarf (radius $\sim 10^4 \text{ km}$) collapses into a neutron star (radius $\sim 10 \text{ km}$), the radius decreases by a factor of $\sim 10^3$.
    • Since $B \propto 1/A \propto 1/R^2$, the magnetic field strength increases enormously (by a factor of $\sim (10^3)^2 = 10^6$).
    • The properties and behavior of neutron stars (e.g., pulsars) are significantly influenced by these immense magnetic fields.

  • Degenerate Fermi Gas Model for Stars (Detailed):

    • Consider a white dwarf composed of atoms with mass number $A$. Let $x = Z/A$ be the fraction of protons (and electrons) per nucleon.
    • Total number of nucleons is $n_{total}$. Number of electrons $n_e = x n_{total}$.
    • The total energy of the star is the sum of the kinetic energy of the degenerate electrons and the gravitational potential energy: $$ E(R) = E_{KE} - E_{grav} = n_e \langle E_e \rangle - \frac{3}{5} \frac{GM^2}{R} $$
      • For non-relativistic degenerate electrons, $\langle E_e \rangle \propto (n_e/V)^{2/3} \propto n_e^{2/3}/R^2$.
      • $E_{KE} = n_e \frac{3}{5} \frac{p_F^2}{2m_e} = n_e \frac{3}{5} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e} (3\pi^2 n_e)^{2/3}$.
      • Substituting $n_e = x N / V = x M / (m_n V)$: $$ E(R) = \frac{3}{5} x M \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e} \left(\frac{9\pi}{4}\right)^{2/3} \left(\frac{x}{m_n}\right)^{2/3} \frac{1}{R^2} - \frac{3}{5} \frac{GM^2}{R} $$
    • Equilibrium Radius ($R_{eq}$): To find the stable equilibrium radius, set $\frac{dE}{dR} = 0$. $$ \frac{dE}{dR} = -2 (\text{KE term}) \frac{1}{R^3} + (\text{Grav term}) \frac{1}{R^2} = 0 $$ Solving for $R$ yields an equilibrium radius. The notes give $R_{eq} \sim 800 \text{ km}$ for a white dwarf.

  • Relativistic Correction for White Dwarfs:

    • If electrons become highly relativistic ($E_e \approx p_F c$), the kinetic energy term changes to $E_{KE} \propto p_F c \propto (n_e)^{1/3} \propto 1/R$.
    • In this relativistic limit, the energy expression becomes: $$ E(R) = C_1 \frac{M^{4/3}}{R} - C_2 \frac{M^2}{R} $$ where $C_1$ and $C_2$ are constants.
    • If $C_1 M^{4/3} = C_2 M^2$, then $E(R)$ is independent of $R$. If $C_1 M^{4/3} > C_2 M^2$, the star expands. If $C_1 M^{4/3} < C_2 M^2$, the star contracts.
    • Setting $dE/dR = 0$ will not give a stable configuration as both terms have the same $1/R$ dependence. Instead, there's a critical mass where the terms balance.
    • This critical mass is the Chandrasekhar limit. Beyond this limit, the star will collapse, as the gravitational term (proportional to $M^2$) will always overcome the degeneracy pressure term (proportional to $M^{4/3}$) for large enough $M$.
    • The notes show a critical density $n_c \sim 10^{37}$ leading to $M \sim 1.4 \text{ M}_\odot$.

  • "Bold Extrapolation" (Nuclear Physics to Stellar Objects):

    • The notes suggest adding a gravitational term to the SEMF: $$ BE(A,Z) = a_v A - a_s A^{2/3} - a_c \frac{Z(Z-1)}{A^{1/3}} - a_a \frac{(A-2Z)^2}{A} \pm a_p A^{-k} + \frac{3}{5}\frac{GM^2}{R} $$
    • For very large $A$, the surface, Coulomb, asymmetry, and pairing terms become relatively small compared to volume and gravitational terms.
    • Approximate: $BE(A,Z) \approx a_v A - a_a A + \frac{3}{5}\frac{G(A m_n)^2}{R_0 A^{1/3}}$. (Assuming $Z \approx A/2$ for simplicity for $a_a$ term effect).
    • For BE to be positive (bound system), $a_v A - a_a A + \frac{3}{5}\frac{G(A m_n)^2}{R_0 A^{1/3}} > 0$.
    • This yields very large $A \sim 10^{57}$ (the number of nucleons in a star), $R \sim 10 \text{ km}$ (neutron star size), and $M \sim 0.1 \text{ M}_\odot$. This conceptually links the nuclear SEMF to the stability of dense stellar objects.
Lecture 12: September 9, 2023 - Magic Numbers & Nuclear Shell Model

Lecture 12: September 9, 2023, Saturday

  • Magic Numbers:

    • Nuclei with specific numbers of protons ($Z$) or neutrons ($N$) are exceptionally stable. These "magic numbers" are: 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126.
    • They are analogous to the noble gas configurations in atomic physics (closed shells).
    • This phenomenon is explained by the Nuclear Shell Model.

  • Nuclear Shell Model (Maria Mayer & Hans Jensen, 1963):

    • Treats nucleons (protons and neutrons) as independent particles moving in an average potential created by all other nucleons in the nucleus.
    • Woods-Saxon Potential ($V(r)$): A commonly used realistic average potential that better describes the nuclear potential than a simple harmonic oscillator or square well. $$ V(r) = \frac{-V_0}{1 + e^{(r-R)/a}} $$ (Where $V_0$ is the depth, $R$ is the radius, $a$ is the diffuseness).

  • SchrÃ¶dinger Equation for Central Potential:

    • The time-independent SchrÃ¶dinger equation for a nucleon in a central potential $U(r)$: $$ \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + U(r)\right] \Phi(\vec{r}) = E\Phi(\vec{r}) $$
    • Separation of Variables: The wave function $\Phi(\vec{r})$ can be separated into a radial part $R(r)$ and an angular part $F(\theta,\phi)$: $$ \Phi(\vec{r}) = R(r) F(\theta,\phi) $$
    • The Laplacian operator $\nabla^2$ in spherical coordinates is: $$ \nabla^2 = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\right) + \frac{1}{r^2} \left[\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\right) + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\phi^2}\right] $$ The angular part is related to the square of the orbital angular momentum operator $L^2$: $$ L^2 = -\hbar^2 \left[\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\right) + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\phi^2}\right] $$
    • Substituting this into the SchrÃ¶dinger equation and separating variables leads to:
      • Angular Equation: $$ \frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial F}{\partial\theta}\right) + \frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial\phi^2} = -l(l+1)F $$ The solutions are spherical harmonics $Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$, and the eigenvalues are $l(l+1)\hbar^2$ for $L^2$. $$ \frac{d^2\Phi}{d\phi^2} = -m^2\Phi \implies \Phi(\phi) = Ce^{im\phi} $$ The quantum number $m$ must be an integer (for periodicity, $\Phi(\phi+2\pi) = \Phi(\phi)$). For a given $l$, $m$ ranges from $-l$ to $+l$.
      • Radial Equation: $$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d}{dr}\left(r^2\frac{dR}{dr}\right) + \left[U(r) + \frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2mr^2}\right] R(r) = E R(r) $$ The term $\frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2mr^2}$ acts as an effective potential (centrifugal barrier). This is a general equation for central potentials. For a hydrogen atom, $U(r) = -e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$.

  • Infinite Spherical Well Model:

    • A simple potential: $U(r) = 0$ for $r \le R$, $U(r) = \infty$ for $r > R$.
    • The radial equation becomes solvable using spherical Bessel functions. The energy eigenvalues $E_{nl}$ are determined by the boundary condition that $R(R)=0$.
    • The quantum numbers are $n$ (principal, similar to atomic, $n=1,2,\dots$) and $l$ (orbital angular momentum, $l=0,1,2,\dots$).
    • The energy levels are $E_{nl} = \frac{\hbar^2 k_{nl}^2}{2m}$, where $k_{nl}$ are related to the zeros of the spherical Bessel functions.
    • Level Scheme and Degeneracy (from notes):
      • 1s (l=0, n=1): 2 nucleons (2j+1 = 2(0)+1 = 1, but 2 for spin degeneracy) -> Cumulative: 2
      • 1p (l=1, n=1): 6 nucleons (2(1)+1 = 3 states, $\times 2$ for spin) -> Cumulative: 8
      • 1d (l=2, n=1): 10 nucleons -> Cumulative: 18
      • 2s (l=0, n=2): 2 nucleons -> Cumulative: 20
      • 1f (l=3, n=1): 14 nucleons -> Cumulative: 34
      • 2p (l=1, n=2): 6 nucleons -> Cumulative: 40
    • Conclusion: This model only partially explains magic numbers (2, 8, 20 are reproduced), but fails for others (28, 50, 82, 126 are not explained by this simple model's shell gaps).

  • 3D Isotropic Harmonic Oscillator Model:

    • Potential: $U(r) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 r^2$.
    • Energy eigenvalues: $E = \hbar\omega (N + 3/2)$, where $N = n_x + n_y + n_z = 0, 1, 2, \dots$.
    • The relationship between the quantum numbers for the harmonic oscillator ($N$) and for central potentials ($n,l$) is $N = 2(n-1) + l$.
    • Level Scheme and Degeneracy:
      • $N=0$: $1s$ (l=0, n=1). Degeneracy = 2. Cumulative: 2.
      • $N=1$: $1p$ (l=1, n=1). Degeneracy = 6. Cumulative: 8.
      • $N=2$: $2s$ (l=0, n=2), $1d$ (l=2, n=1). Degeneracy = 2 + 10 = 12. Cumulative: 20.
      • $N=3$: $2p$ (l=1, n=2), $1f$ (l=3, n=1). Degeneracy = 6 + 14 = 20. Cumulative: 40.
    • Conclusion: Similar to the infinite well, this model explains 2, 8, 20, but not the higher magic numbers (28, 50, 82, 126). The problem lies in the degeneracies at higher $N$.
Lecture 13: September 12, 2023 - Spin-Orbit Coupling & Magic Numbers

Lecture 13: September 12, 2023, Tuesday

  • Orbital and Spin Angular Momentum (Spin-Orbit Coupling):

    • The key to explaining all magic numbers is the inclusion of a strong spin-orbit coupling term in the nuclear potential.
    • The Hamiltonian becomes $H = H_0 + f \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$, where $H_0$ is the central potential part (e.g., Woods-Saxon or Harmonic Oscillator) and $f$ is a coupling constant. The term $f \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$ describes the interaction between the nucleon's orbital angular momentum $\vec{L}$ and its intrinsic spin $\vec{S}$.
    • Commuting Operators: While $H_0$ commutes with $L^2, L_z, S^2, S_z$, the full Hamiltonian $H$ with the $\vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$ term does not commute with $L_z$ or $S_z$ separately. However, it does commute with the total angular momentum $\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}$ (and $J^2$, $J_z$). So, $H, J^2, J_z, L^2, S^2$ form a complete set of commuting operators.

  • Expectation Value of $\vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$:

    • We know $\vec{J}^2 = (\vec{L} + \vec{S})^2 = \vec{L}^2 + \vec{S}^2 + 2\vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$.
    • So, $\vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} = \frac{1}{2}(\vec{J}^2 - \vec{L}^2 - \vec{S}^2)$.
    • The expectation value is: $$ \langle \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} \rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2} [j(j+1) - l(l+1) - s(s+1)] $$
    • For a nucleon, spin $s = 1/2$, so $s(s+1) = (1/2)(3/2) = 3/4$.
    • The total angular momentum $j$ can take two values: $j = l + 1/2$ or $j = l - 1/2$.
    • Case 1: $j = l + 1/2$: $$ \langle \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} \rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2} \left[ \left(l+\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(l+\frac{3}{2}\right) - l(l+1) - \frac{3}{4} \right] = \frac{\hbar^2 l}{2} $$
    • Case 2: $j = l - 1/2$ (for $l \ge 1$, as $j$ cannot be negative): $$ \langle \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} \rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2} \left[ \left(l-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(l+\frac{1}{2}\right) - l(l+1) - \frac{3}{4} \right] = -\frac{\hbar^2 (l+1)}{2} $$

  • Energy Splitting Due to Spin-Orbit Coupling:

    • The energy correction to the levels is $\Delta E = f \langle \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} \rangle$.
    • It is empirically found that the spin-orbit coupling in nuclei is negative ($f < 0$). This means states with larger $j$ (which have positive $\langle \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} \rangle$) are shifted down in energy, becoming more stable. States with smaller $j$ (negative $\langle \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S} \rangle$) are shifted up in energy.
    • Experimentally observed: Lower $j$ value leads to higher energy, higher $j$ value leads to lower energy.
    • The energy splitting between the two states is: $$ \Delta E = E_{j=l-1/2} - E_{j=l+1/2} = f \left( -\frac{\hbar^2(l+1)}{2} - \frac{\hbar^2 l}{2} \right) = -f \frac{\hbar^2(2l+1)}{2} $$ Since $f < 0$, $\Delta E > 0$, meaning the $j=l-1/2$ state is indeed at higher energy. This splitting is called "fine structure."

  • Impact on Magic Numbers:

    • This spin-orbit splitting creates significant energy gaps between shells that were degenerate or closely spaced in the simple harmonic oscillator or square well models.
    • Crucially, large splitting occurs for high $l$ values. For example, the $1f_{7/2}$ state (from $N=3$ shell, $l=3, j=3+1/2=7/2$) drops significantly in energy, falling into the previous shell and creating a gap at $N=28$.
    • Similarly, other large splittings (e.g., $1g_{9/2}$ dropping) explain the magic numbers 50, 82, and 126.
    • The shell model with strong spin-orbit coupling successfully reproduces all the experimentally observed magic numbers.

  • Harmonic Oscillator Levels with Spin-Orbit Coupling (Detailed Level Scheme):

    • Recall the Harmonic Oscillator levels: $N = 2(n-1) + l$.
    • Each $(n,l)$ state splits into $j=l+1/2$ and $j=l-1/2$ levels due to $H = H_0 + f \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}$, where $f<0$. The $j=l+1/2$ state is lower in energy.

Lecture 16: October 10, 2023 - Nuclear Spin & Magnetic Moment

Lecture 16: October 10, 2023, Tuesday

  • Total Angular Momentum of Nucleus ($I$):

    • The total angular momentum of a nucleus (its nuclear spin) is denoted by $\vec{I}$. It is the vector sum of the orbital angular momenta ($\vec{L}$) and intrinsic spins ($\vec{S}$) of all nucleons.
    • For a single nucleon, $\vec{j} = \vec{l} + \vec{s}$. The magnitude of $j$ is quantized such that $j(j+1)\hbar^2$.
    • For the entire nucleus, $\vec{I} = \sum \vec{j}_i$ (coupling of individual nucleon angular momenta). The notes show $I = |\vec{L} + \vec{S}|$ for simplified cases (e.g., odd-A nuclei, where $L$ and $S$ refer to the unpaired nucleon).
    • The projection of $I$ on the z-axis is $M_I = -I, -I+1, \dots, +I$.

  • Spin Parity of Nucleus ($I^\pi$):

    • The nuclear state is characterized by its total angular momentum $I$ and its parity $\pi$.
    • The parity operator $\hat{P}$ acts on the spatial part of the wave function: $\hat{P} \Psi(\vec{r}) = \Psi(-\vec{r}) = (-1)^l \Psi(\vec{r})$.
    • The parity of the nuclear state is determined by the sum of the orbital angular momenta of all nucleons: $\pi = (-1)^{\sum l_i}$. For odd-A nuclei, it's typically determined by the parity of the unpaired nucleon $(-1)^l$.

  • Even-Even (EE) Nuclei:

    • In their ground state, all protons and neutrons pair up. Each pair has a total angular momentum of zero.
    • Therefore, the total angular momentum of the ground state is $I = 0$.
    • The ground state parity is always positive ($\pi = +1$). So, the ground state spin-parity is $0^+$.
    • This pairing significantly reduces the energy, contributing to their high stability.

  • Even-Odd (EO) or Odd-Even (OE) Nuclei:

    • These nuclei have one unpaired nucleon. The total nuclear spin-parity is determined by the angular momentum and parity of this single unpaired nucleon.
    • Example: For $^{15}_{7}\text{N}_8$ (Odd Z, Even N), the 7th proton is unpaired. Its spin-parity determines the nuclear spin-parity. From shell model, $1p_{1/2}$ shell is partially filled for protons. So, it's $1/2^-$.
    • The notes mention $^{61}_{28}\text{Ni}_{33}$ is an exception where one expects $1/2^+$ but actually observed $5/2^-$. This highlights complexities and the need for more sophisticated models (e.g., configuration mixing).
    • Excited States: Show different spin-parities, for instance, $5/2^+$ as ground state, $1/2^+$ as first excited state, etc.

  • Odd-Odd (OO) Nuclei:

    • Both a proton and a neutron are unpaired. Their angular momenta couple to determine the total nuclear spin ($I$).
    • j-j coupling (Brennan-Bernstein Rules): These rules are empirical guidelines for predicting the ground state spin $I$ of odd-odd nuclei based on the angular momenta ($j_p, j_n$) of the last unpaired proton and neutron.
      • Rule 1: If $j_p = l_p \pm s_p$ and $j_n = l_n \mp s_n$ (i.e., one is from $l+1/2$ subshell and the other from $l-1/2$ subshell), then $I = |j_p - j_n|$.
      • Rule 2: If $j_p = l_p \pm s_p$ and $j_n = l_n \pm s_n$ (i.e., both are from $l+1/2$ or both from $l-1/2$ subshells), then $I = j_p + j_n$ (though other values like $|j_p - j_n|$ are possible).
      • Parity is $\pi = (-1)^{l_p + l_n}$.
      • Example: If $j_n=7/2$ ($l_n=3$) and $j_p=3/2$ ($l_p=1$). If rule 1 applies, $I = |7/2 - 3/2| = 2$. Parity $= (-1)^{3+1} = (-1)^4 = +1$. So, $I^\pi = 2^+$.

  • Magnetic Moment ($\mu$):

    • Measurement: Can be measured experimentally (e.g., using Zeeman effect).
    • Origin: Arises from the orbital angular momentum of protons and the intrinsic spins of both protons and neutrons.
    • Classical Analog: For a current loop, $\mu = IA = I \pi r^2 = (ev/2\pi r) \pi r^2 = evr/2$.
    • Quantum Mechanical Expression: $$ \vec{\mu} = g_l \frac{e\hbar}{2m} \frac{\vec{L}}{\hbar} + g_s \frac{e\hbar}{2m} \frac{\vec{S}}{\hbar} $$ Or, in terms of nuclear magnetons ($\mu_N = \frac{e\hbar}{2m_p}$): $$ \vec{\mu} = g_l \mu_N \frac{\vec{L}}{\hbar} + g_s \mu_N \frac{\vec{S}}{\hbar} $$
      • $g_l$: Orbital g-factor ($g_l = 1$ for protons, $g_l = 0$ for neutrons, as they are neutral).
      • $g_s$: Spin g-factor ($g_s^p \approx 5.586$ for proton, $g_s^n \approx -3.826$ for neutron). These are anomalous values, not simply 2.

  • Zeeman Effect:

    • Applying an external magnetic field $\vec{B}$ introduces a perturbation to the Hamiltonian: $H_1 = -\vec{\mu} \cdot \vec{B}$.
    • This causes splitting of nuclear energy levels based on the projection of the total angular momentum ($M_I$) along the magnetic field direction.
    • The energy shift is $\Delta E = - \langle \mu_z \rangle B_z = -g_I \mu_N M_I B_z$, where $g_I$ is the nuclear g-factor for the state.
    • This allows experimental determination of the magnetic moment and $g_I$.

  • Magnetic Moment of a Nucleon (Recap):

    • Orbital magnetic moment: $\mu_l = g_l \frac{L}{\hbar} \mu_N$. ($g_l=1$ for proton, $0$ for neutron).
    • Spin magnetic moment: $\mu_s = g_s \frac{S}{\hbar} \mu_N$. ($g_s^p \approx 5.586$, $g_s^n \approx -3.826$).
    • For electrons, $\mu_e = g_l \frac{L}{\hbar} \mu_B + g_s \frac{S}{\hbar} \mu_B$. Here, $\mu_B$ is the Bohr magneton.

  • Total Nuclear Magnetic Moment ($\mu$):

    • For a state with total angular momentum $\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}$: $$ \vec{\mu} = g_L \vec{L} \mu_N + g_S \vec{S} \mu_N $$ where $g_L$ and $g_S$ are the effective orbital and spin g-factors for the nucleus.
    • The expectation value of $\vec{\mu}$ along the direction of $\vec{J}$ is the magnetic moment of the nucleus: $$ \mu = \langle \vec{\mu} \cdot \hat{J} \rangle = g_J J \mu_N $$ Where $g_J$ is the Lande g-factor for the nucleus.
    • Derivation of Lande g-factor ($g_J$): The notes present the formula for $g_J$ for a state with given $l$, $s$, and $j$: $$ g_J = g_l \frac{j(j+1) + l(l+1) - s(s+1)}{2j(j+1)} + g_s \frac{j(j+1) - l(l+1) + s(s+1)}{2j(j+1)} $$ This formula holds for a single particle in a shell model.

  • Calculations for Specific States (Even-Odd Nuclei):

    • Even-Even Nuclei: Ground state $0^+$, so total angular momentum $J=0$, leading to $\mu = 0$.
    • Even-Odd Nuclei (Magnetic Moment determined by single unpaired nucleon):
      • Consider an unpaired nucleon in a state with total angular momentum $j = l \pm 1/2$. The $z$-component of total angular momentum is $m_j$.
      • The expectation value of the magnetic moment in the $m_j = j$ state is $\mu = \langle j, j | \mu_z | j, j \rangle$.
      • For $j = l + 1/2$: $$ \mu = \left(g_l l + g_s \frac{1}{2}\right) \mu_N $$ This is the Schmidt limit for $j=l+1/2$.
      • For $j = l - 1/2$: The state is a superposition of $|m_l=l, m_s=-1/2\rangle$ and $|m_l=l-1, m_s=1/2\rangle$. $$ |j=l-1/2, m_j=l-1/2\rangle = a |l, l-1/2\rangle + b |l-1, l+1/2\rangle $$ The coefficients $a$ and $b$ are determined by angular momentum coupling (Clebsch-Gordan coefficients). $|a|^2 = \frac{l+1}{2l+1}$ and $|b|^2 = \frac{l}{2l+1}$. The expectation value of $\mu_z$ for this state is: $$ \mu = \left[\frac{l+1}{2l+1}\left(g_l l - \frac{g_s}{2}\right) + \frac{l}{2l+1}\left(g_l (l-1) + \frac{g_s}{2}\right)\right] \mu_N $$ This is the Schmidt limit for $j=l-1/2$.
      • Example (Deuteron): The notes mentioned that the deuteron's magnetic moment (0.857 $\mu_N$) could be explained by a mixture of S-wave (l=0, S=1) and D-wave (l=2, S=1) states. This indicates that the simple single-particle shell model is insufficient and that configuration mixing is important.

  • Electric Dipole Moment of Nuclei:

    • The electric dipole moment of nuclei is zero. This is a consequence of parity conservation. If a nucleus had a permanent electric dipole moment, its energy in an electric field would be $E = -\vec{d} \cdot \vec{E}$. Under parity transformation, $\vec{d} \rightarrow -\vec{d}$ and $\vec{E} \rightarrow -\vec{E}$, so $E$ is invariant. However, if the ground state itself possessed a dipole moment, it would mean that the ground state is not a state of definite parity, which contradicts the observed parity conservation of the strong and electromagnetic interactions. (This applies to fundamental particles as well, and would imply CP violation).

Lecture 18: October 14, 2023, Saturday - Nuclear Vibration & Rotation

  • Nuclear Shell Model (Limitations):

    • While the shell model successfully describes properties like spin-parity for ground states of odd-A nuclei (based on the single unpaired nucleon), it cannot fully explain the collective behavior seen in the excited states of many nuclei, especially even-even nuclei.
    • Excited States of Even-Even Nuclei:
      • Ground state is always $0^+$.
      • Often, a low-lying first excited state is $2^+$. Its energy varies: $\sim 1 \text{ MeV}$ for lighter/spherical nuclei ($A < 150$) and $\sim 100 \text{ keV}$ for heavier/deformed nuclei ($A > 150$).
      • Higher excited states often follow a characteristic pattern: $4^+, 6^+, \dots$
      • The ratio of energies $E(4^+)/E(2^+)$ is $\approx 2$ for $A < 150$ (vibrational nuclei) and $\approx 3.33$ for $A > 150$ (rotational nuclei).
      • Electric Quadrupole Moment (EQM) is near zero for $A < 150$ (suggesting spherical shape) and finite (large) for $A > 150$ (suggesting deformation).

  • Explanation: Nuclear Vibrations:

    • To explain the $E(4^+)/E(2^+) \approx 2$ ratio and near-spherical shapes, the concept of nuclear surface vibrations is introduced.
    • The nuclear surface can oscillate around its equilibrium spherical shape.
    • The nuclear radius $R(\theta,\phi,t)$ can be described as an average radius $R_{avg}$ plus a sum of spherical harmonics with time-dependent amplitudes $\alpha_{\lambda\mu}(t)$: $$ R(\theta,\phi,t) = R_{avg} + \sum_{\lambda,\mu} \alpha_{\lambda\mu}(t) Y_{\lambda\mu}(\theta,\phi) $$
      • $\lambda=0$: Monopole vibration (breathing mode, spherical shape maintained).
      • $\lambda=1$: Dipole vibration (center of mass oscillation, not directly associated with nuclear force states).
      • $\lambda=2$: Quadrupole vibration (non-spherical deformation, causes $2^+$ states).
      • $\lambda=3$: Octupole vibration (pear-shaped deformation, causes $3^-$ states).
    • Phonons: These vibrations are quantized, and their quanta are called "phonons." Each phonon carries a specific angular momentum and parity ($\lambda^\pi$).
    • Excited States as Phonons:
      • A $2^+$ state can be interpreted as the $0^+$ ground state plus one quadrupole phonon ($1\hbar\omega$ energy, $\lambda=2$).
      • Two quadrupole phonons can couple to states with total angular momentum $I = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4$. However, due to Bose-Einstein statistics (phonons are bosons), and the requirement for definite parity and symmetry, only even angular momenta are allowed when two identical quadrupole phonons couple ($I = 0^+, 2^+, 4^+$).
      • The energy of two-phonon states is approximately $2\hbar\omega$, three-phonon states $3\hbar\omega$, etc. This leads to the $E(4^+)/E(2^+) \approx 2$ ratio (as $E(2^+) \approx \hbar\omega$, $E(4^+) \approx 2\hbar\omega$).
    • This model explains the energy pattern of vibrational nuclei.

  • Explanation: Nuclear Rotations:

    • For nuclei with $A > 150$, the $E(4^+)/E(2^+) \approx 3.33$ ratio and large EQM suggest that these nuclei are permanently deformed (e.g., prolate or oblate spheroids) and can undergo collective rotations.
    • Rotational Energy in Quantum Mechanics: For a quantum rotor, the energy levels are given by: $$ E(I) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2\mathcal{I}} I(I+1) $$ where $\mathcal{I}$ is the moment of inertia and $I$ is the rotational angular momentum quantum number.
    • Allowed States: For Even-Even nuclei, only even values of $I$ are allowed in rotational bands ($I = 0, 2, 4, 6, \dots$). This is because of the symmetry properties of the nuclear wave function, as rotation by $180^\circ$ around an axis of symmetry should leave the state unchanged.
    • Ratio of Energies: $$ \frac{E(4^+)}{E(2^+)} = \frac{\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mathcal{I}} 4(4+1)}{\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mathcal{I}} 2(2+1)} = \frac{4 \times 5}{2 \times 3} = \frac{20}{6} = 3.33 $$ This theoretical ratio exactly matches the experimental observation for rotational nuclei, providing strong evidence for collective nuclear rotation.
    • Moment of Inertia ($\mathcal{I}$):
      • From the observed energies, the moment of inertia can be extracted.
      • It is found that $\mathcal{I}_{\text{rigid}} > \mathcal{I}_{\text{observed}} > \mathcal{I}_{\text{fluid}}$. This indicates that nuclear matter is neither a perfectly rigid rotor nor an ideal fluid, but rather has a "soft" or "viscous" character.

Lecture 20: October 26, 2023 - Nuclear Force Properties & Deuteron

Lecture 20: October 26, 2023, Thursday - Nuclear Force

  • Characteristics of Nuclear Force:

    • Not a Fundamental Force: It is a residual strong force (similar to Van der Waals forces, which are residual electromagnetic forces). The fundamental strong force binds quarks together.
    • Short-Range: Acts over distances of a few femtometers ($\text{fm}$). Becomes negligible beyond $\sim 2 \text{ fm}$.
    • Strong: Much stronger than the Coulomb repulsion at short distances.
    • Attractive and Repulsive (complex potential):
      • It is attractive at intermediate ranges ($\approx 0.5 \text{ fm}$ to $2 \text{ fm}$), reaching a maximum attraction around $1 \text{ fm}$.
      • It becomes strongly repulsive at very short distances ($r \lesssim 0.5 \text{ fm}$). This "hard core repulsion" prevents the nucleus from collapsing.
    • Charge Independent: The strong force interaction between any two nucleons is approximately the same, regardless of their charge (i.e., proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and proton-neutron interactions are similar in strength, ignoring electromagnetic effects). This is a manifestation of isospin symmetry.
    • Spin Dependent: The strength of the nuclear force depends on the relative spin orientations of the interacting nucleons. For example, the deuteron (np) exists in a spin-aligned state ($S=1$).
    • Non-Central Component (Tensor Force): The nuclear force is not purely central. It has a non-central (tensor) component that depends on the orientation of the nucleon spins relative to the vector connecting them. This implies that orbital angular momentum is not always conserved in nuclear interactions, though total angular momentum is.

  • Deuteron (Deuterium Nucleus, $^2_1\text{H}$ or $D$):

    • The simplest bound nucleus, consisting of one proton and one neutron.
    • **(i) Binding Energy**: $BE = (m_p + m_n - m_D)c^2 \approx 2.225 \text{ MeV}$. (So, binding energy per nucleon $\approx 1.1 \text{ MeV}$).
    • **(ii) Charge Radius**: $\approx 2.13 \text{ fm}$.
    • **(iii) Spin Parity**: $1^+$ (Ground state). This means total angular momentum $I=1$ and positive parity.
    • **(iv) No Excited State Found**: The deuteron has no stable excited states; it breaks apart easily.
    • **(v) Magnetic Dipole Moment**: $\mu_D \approx 0.857 \mu_N$. This value is close to, but not exactly, the sum of proton and neutron magnetic moments, indicating a more complex structure.
    • **(vi) Electric Quadrupole Moment (Q)**: $Q = 0.00288 \text{ barn}$ (non-zero).
      • A non-zero quadrupole moment implies that the deuteron is not perfectly spherically symmetric; it has a slight cigar-like (prolate) deformation. This is crucial evidence for the tensor component of the nuclear force.

  • Crude Approximation: Square Well Potential for Deuteron:

    • We can model the np interaction with a square well potential: $$ V(r) = -V_0 \quad \text{for } r < r_0 \quad (\text{where } r_0 \approx 2.13 \text{ fm}) $$ $$ V(r) = 0 \quad \text{for } r > r_0 $$
    • Center of Mass (COM) Frame: To simplify, we work in the COM frame, where the two-body problem reduces to a single particle of reduced mass $\mu = \frac{m_p m_n}{m_p + m_n}$ moving in the potential.
    • SchrÃ¶dinger Equation in COM frame: $$ \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V(r)\right] \psi(\vec{r}) = E\psi(\vec{r}) $$
    • Radial Equation for $u(r) = rR(r)$: (Assuming $l=0$ for simplicity first, as deuteron is mostly S-wave) $$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\frac{d^2u}{dr^2} + V(r)u(r) = E u(r) $$
    • Solutions:
      • Region I ($r < r_0$): $V(r) = -V_0$. $$ \frac{d^2u}{dr^2} + \frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}(E+V_0)u = 0 $$ Let $k_1^2 = \frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}(E+V_0)$. Solution: $u_1(r) = A\sin(k_1 r) + B\cos(k_1 r)$. Since $u(0)=0$ (because $R(0)$ must be finite, so $rR(r) \rightarrow 0$ as $r \rightarrow 0$), $B=0$. So, $u_1(r) = A\sin(k_1 r)$.
      • Region II ($r > r_0$): $V(r) = 0$. $$ \frac{d^2u}{dr^2} + \frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}E u = 0 $$ Let $-k_2^2 = \frac{2\mu E}{\hbar^2}$ (since $E<0$ for bound state, $k_2$ is real). Solution: $u_2(r) = Ce^{-k_2 r} + De^{k_2 r}$. Since $u(r) \rightarrow 0$ as $r \rightarrow \infty$ for a bound state, $D=0$. So, $u_2(r) = Ce^{-k_2 r}$.
    • Boundary Conditions at $r = r_0$:
      1. Continuity of $u(r)$: $u_1(r_0) = u_2(r_0) \implies A\sin(k_1 r_0) = Ce^{-k_2 r_0}$.
      2. Continuity of $du/dr$: $u_1'(r_0) = u_2'(r_0) \implies Ak_1\cos(k_1 r_0) = -Ck_2 e^{-k_2 r_0}$.
    • Transcendental Equation: Dividing the second equation by the first: $$ k_1 \cot(k_1 r_0) = -k_2 $$ This is a transcendental equation that relates $V_0$, $E$, and $r_0$. It can be solved graphically to find the depth of the potential well $V_0 \approx 35 \text{ MeV}$.

      • Total spin $S=0$ (singlet) or $S=1$ (triplet).
    • For $S=0$:
      • $l=0 \implies I=0$. Parity $(-1)^0 = +1$. So $0^+$.
      • $l=1 \implies I=1$. Parity $(-1)^1 = -1$. So $1^-$.
      • $l=2 \implies I=2$. Parity $(-1)^2 = +1$. So $2^+$.
    • For $S=1$:
      • $l=0 \implies I=1$. Parity $(-1)^0 = +1$. So $1^+$. (This is the dominant part of deuteron's ground state).
      • $l=1 \implies I=0,1,2$. Parity $(-1)^1 = -1$. So $0^-, 1^-, 2^-$.
      • $l=2 \implies I=1,2,3$. Parity $(-1)^2 = +1$. So $1^+, 2^+, 3^+$. (This includes the D-wave contribution).

  • Nuclear Decay (General Principles):

    • Reaction $Q$-value: For a nuclear reaction or decay $i \rightarrow f$, the $Q$-value is defined as: $$ Q = (M_{\text{initial}} - M_{\text{final}})c^2 $$ Where $M_{\text{initial}}$ is the total mass of reactants and $M_{\text{final}}$ is the total mass of products.
    • Condition for Allowed Reaction: A reaction or decay is allowed (energetically possible) only if $Q > 0$. If $Q < 0$, the reaction is forbidden unless external energy is supplied.
    • Example: Neutron Decay (recap): $n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$.

      • Since $Q > 0$, the decay is allowed.

    • For a two-body decay $X \rightarrow Y + \alpha$ (parent nucleus $X$ decaying into daughter $Y$ and $\alpha$-particle), assuming the parent is initially at rest, by conservation of momentum: $$ \vec{p}_Y + \vec{p}_\alpha = 0 \implies |\vec{p}_Y| = |\vec{p}_\alpha| = p $$

Lecture 22: November 2, 2023 - Alpha Decay & Gamow's Theory

Lecture 22: November 2, 2023, Thursday

  • Radioactive Decay (Recap):

    • Lifetime Equation: $N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$, where $N(t)$ is the number of undecayed nuclei at time $t$, $N_0$ is the initial number, and $\lambda$ is the decay constant.
    • Half-life ($t_{1/2}$): The time it takes for half of the nuclei to decay. $$ N_0/2 = N_0 e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} \implies 1/2 = e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} \implies \ln(1/2) = -\lambda t_{1/2} $$ $$ t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} $$
    • Activity ($A$): The rate of decay (number of decays per unit time). $$ A = \left|\frac{dN}{dt}\right| = \lambda N_0 e^{-\lambda t} = \lambda N(t) $$

  • Geiger-Nuttall Law:

    • An empirical relationship observed for alpha decay: $$ \ln(t_{1/2}) = a + b/\sqrt{Q} $$ where $a$ and $b$ are constants for a series of alpha emitters, and $Q$ is the alpha decay energy. This law shows that alpha emitters with higher decay energies ($Q$) have much shorter half-lives.

  • Gamow's Theory of Alpha Decay (Quantum Tunneling):

    • Gamow explained the Geiger-Nuttall law using quantum mechanics, specifically the concept of quantum tunneling through a potential barrier.
    • Model:
      1. An $\alpha$-particle is assumed to exist as a pre-formed entity inside the nucleus.
      2. Inside the nucleus ($r < R$, where $R$ is nuclear radius), it experiences the attractive strong nuclear potential (a "potential well").
      3. Outside the nucleus ($r > R$), it experiences the repulsive Coulomb potential from the remaining $(Z-2)$ protons of the daughter nucleus. This creates a potential barrier: $$ V_c(r) = \frac{(Z-2)2e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} $$ The maximum height of this barrier occurs at the nuclear surface $R$: $V_c(R) = \frac{(Z-2)2e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R}$. For a heavy nucleus like Uranium, this can be $\sim 24 \text{ MeV}$.
      4. The alpha particle has kinetic energy equal to the $Q$-value (typically $5 \text{ MeV}$), which is less than the barrier height. Classically, it cannot escape.
      5. Quantum mechanically, there's a finite probability for the $\alpha$-particle to "tunnel" through this barrier.
    • Tunneling Probability ($P$): For a barrier $V(r)$, the tunneling probability is approximated by $P \approx e^{-2G}$, where $G$ is the Gamow factor: $$ G = \sqrt{\frac{2m_\alpha}{\hbar^2}} \int_R^b \sqrt{V_c(r) - Q} dr $$ Here, $b$ is the classical turning point where $V_c(b) = Q$. Solving the integral for the Coulomb barrier: $$ G = \frac{(Z-2)e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar} \sqrt{\frac{2m_\alpha}{Q}} \left[ \frac{\pi}{2} - \arcsin\left(\sqrt{\frac{R}{b}}\right) - \sqrt{\frac{R}{b}\left(1-\frac{R}{b}\right)} \right] $$ For $R \ll b$ (valid for most alpha decays), this simplifies.
    • The decay constant $\lambda$ is given by: $$ \lambda = f P $$ where $f$ is the frequency with which the $\alpha$-particle "assaults" the barrier (attempts to escape). This is roughly $f \approx v_\alpha/(2R) \approx 10^{21} \text{ times/s}$.
    • Example Calculation: Using $Q \sim 5 \text{ MeV}$, $V_0 \sim 40 \text{ MeV}$ (potential well depth), and $f \sim 10^{21} \text{ Hz}$, the tunneling probability $P$ can be extremely small (e.g., $e^{-2G} \sim 10^{-39}$).
    • This leads to a very small decay constant $\lambda \sim 10^{21} \times 10^{-39} = 10^{-18} \text{ s}^{-1}$.
    • And a very long half-life: $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda \sim \ln 2 / 10^{-18} \approx 0.693 \times 10^{18} \text{ s} \sim 10^{10} \text{ years}$.
    • This beautifully explains the vast range of half-lives observed in alpha decay for relatively small changes in $Q$-value, which is the essence of the Geiger-Nuttall law.
Lecture 23: November 7, 2023 - Fundamental Forces & Particles

Lecture 23: November 7, 2023, Tuesday - Particle Physics

  • Fundamental Particles and Forces (Standard Model):

    • Fermions (Spin 1/2): Matter particles.
      • Quarks: Six flavors (u, d, c, s, t, b) in three generations. They carry "color charge" and interact via the strong force. (u=up, d=down, c=charm, s=strange, t=top, b=bottom).
      • Leptons: Six flavors (e, $\nu_e$, $\mu$, $\nu_\mu$, $\tau$, $\nu_\tau$) in three generations. They do not carry color charge. (e=electron, $\mu$=muon, $\tau$=tau; $\nu$=neutrino). Neutrinos are neutral. Charged leptons interact via EM and weak forces.
        • Only left-handed neutrinos (and right-handed anti-neutrinos) are observed in the Standard Model.
    • Gauge Bosons (Spin 1): Force-carrying particles.
      • Strong Force: Mediated by 8 gluons ($g$). Range is finite due to confinement. Strength $\sim 10^{38}$.
      • Weak Force: Mediated by $W^+, W^-, Z^0$ bosons. Responsible for radioactive decay. Range $\sim 10^{-18} \text{ m}$ (very short due to large mass of bosons, $\sim 100 \text{ GeV}$). Strength $\sim 10^{25}$.
      • Electromagnetic Force: Mediated by photons ($\gamma$). Infinite range (photon is massless). Strength $\sim 10^{36}$.
    • Scalar Boson (Spin 0):
      • Higgs Boson (H): Responsible for giving mass to other fundamental particles.
    • Gravity: Hypothetical graviton. Infinite range (massless). Strength $\sim 1$.

  • Range of Force and Carrier Mass:

    • The range of a force is inversely related to the mass of its force-carrying particle (mediator).
    • Derivation (Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle):
      • Consider a virtual particle of mass $m_X$ exchanged in a force. To create this particle, energy $\Delta E \ge m_X c^2$ must be "borrowed" from vacuum.
      • According to Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, $\Delta E \Delta t \ge \hbar/2$.
      • So, the time over which this virtual particle can exist is $\Delta t \le \hbar / (2m_X c^2)$.
      • The maximum range ($R$) of the force is the distance the virtual particle can travel in this time: $R = c \Delta t \le \frac{\hbar c}{2m_X c^2} = \frac{\hbar}{2m_X c}$.
      • Thus, $R \propto 1/m_X$. If $m_X = 0$ (e.g., photon, graviton), the range is infinite. If $m_X$ is large (e.g., W, Z bosons), the range is very short.

  • Strong Force Confinement:

    • Even though gluons are massless, the strong force is observed only at very short ranges ($\sim 10^{-15} \text{ m}$).
    • This is due to "confinement": quarks and gluons are always confined within hadrons (protons, neutrons, mesons). Free quarks/gluons are never observed. The force between quarks actually increases with distance, preventing their separation.

  • Conservation Laws (Beyond Energy, Momentum, Charge):

    • Certain processes allowed by basic conservation laws are not observed, implying additional conservation laws.
    • Example: $p^+ \rightarrow e^+ + \pi^0$. Allowed by charge, energy-momentum, baryon number (p has B=1, $\pi^0$ has B=0, $e^+$ has B=0). But it's not observed.

  • Baryon Number ($B$):

    • All quarks have baryon number $B = +1/3$. Anti-quarks have $B = -1/3$.
    • Baryons (qqq) have $B=1$.
    • Mesons (q$\bar{\text{q}}$) have $B=0$.
    • Baryon number is conserved in strong, electromagnetic, and weak interactions (no experimental violation observed). This explains why the proton is stable (it's the lightest baryon, so it can't decay into particles with smaller baryon number).
    • Proton decay is predicted by GUTs (Grand Unified Theories) but violates baryon number conservation; it is expected to occur at extremely high energies (beyond current accelerators).

  • Lepton Number ($L$):

    • Each lepton generation has its own conserved lepton number (electron lepton number $L_e$, muon lepton number $L_\mu$, tau lepton number $L_\tau$).
    • Leptons have $L_i = +1$, anti-leptons $L_i = -1$. Other particles have $L_i = 0$.
    • Total lepton number ($L = L_e + L_\mu + L_\tau$) is conserved in all interactions.
    • Example: $e^+ + e^- \rightarrow \pi^+ + \pi^-$. This is not observed.
      • This reaction conserves charge, energy, momentum, and baryon number.
      • However, initial $L_e = +1 + (-1) = 0$. Final $L_e = 0 + 0 = 0$. So it seems allowed. The notes mention that these reactions are "not observed" implying lepton number conservation is not enough.

  • Strangeness ($S$):

    • Introduced to explain "strange particles" (e.g., Kaons, Lambda baryons).
    • These particles are produced by the strong interaction (high cross-section, implying fast production), but they decay by the weak interaction (long lifetime). This seems contradictory initially.
    • Properties of Strange Particles:
      1. Large cross-section (strong production).
      2. Long lifetime (weak decay).
      3. Always produced in pairs ("associated production").
    • Conservation Rule:
      • Strangeness is conserved in strong and electromagnetic interactions.
      • Strangeness is not conserved in weak interactions ($\Delta S = \pm 1$).
    • Example: $\pi^- + p \rightarrow K^0 + \Lambda^0$. This conserves strangeness ($0+0 \rightarrow +1-1 = 0$).
    • Example: $K^0 \rightarrow \pi^+ + \pi^-$. This is a weak decay ($S=1 \rightarrow S=0$).

  • Isospin ($I$):

    • An internal quantum number related to the charge independence of the strong force.
    • Protons and neutrons have very similar masses and strong interactions; they can be considered two states of a single particle, the nucleon.
    • Isospin Doublet: Nucleon ($p, n$) forms an isospin doublet $I=1/2$.
      • $p: I_3 = +1/2$
      • $n: I_3 = -1/2$
    • Isospin Triplet: Pions ($\pi^+, \pi^0, \pi^-$) form an isospin triplet $I=1$.
      • $\pi^+: I_3 = +1$
      • $\pi^0: I_3 = 0$
      • $\pi^-: I_3 = -1$
    • Isospin Quadruplet: Delta baryons ($\Delta^{++}, \Delta^+, \Delta^0, \Delta^-$) form an isospin quadruplet $I=3/2$.

  • Gell-MannâNishijima Formula:

    • Relates charge to isospin, baryon number, and strangeness: $$ Q = I_3 + \frac{B+S}{2} $$
    • This formula (and its extensions to other flavors) is preserved for all particles and helps in classifying hadrons and predicting decays.

  • Isospin (Recap):

    • Isospin is a concept that treats particles with similar strong interactions but different charges (like p and n) as different states of a single particle.
    • Pions form an isospin triplet, which can be thought of as a rotation in an internal isospin space (related to SU(2) symmetry).
    • Hypercharge ($Y$): Introduced as $Y = B+S$. The Gell-MannâNishijima formula can then be written as: $$ Q = I_3 + \frac{Y}{2} $$
    • Determining $I_3$:
      • For proton ($Q=+1, B=1, S=0$): $1 = I_3 + (1+0)/2 \implies I_3 = 1/2$.
      • For neutron ($Q=0, B=1, S=0$): $0 = I_3 + (1+0)/2 \implies I_3 = -1/2$.
      • This confirms $(p, n)$ as an isospin doublet $\begin{pmatrix} p \\ n \end{pmatrix}$.
    • Antiparticles: For antiparticles, the signs of $B, S, C, B'$ are reversed.
      • For anti-proton ($\bar{p}$, $Q=-1, B=-1, S=0$): $-1 = I_3 + (-1+0)/2 \implies I_3 = -1/2$.
      • For anti-neutron ($\bar{n}$, $Q=0, B=-1, S=0$): $0 = I_3 + (-1+0)/2 \implies I_3 = 1/2$.
      • So, $(\bar{n}, \bar{p})$ forms an anti-baryon doublet $\begin{pmatrix} \bar{n} \\ \bar{p} \end{pmatrix}$.
    • For $\Delta^{++}$ ($Q=+2, B=1, S=0$): $2 = I_3 + (1+0)/2 \implies I_3 = 3/2$.
    • For the $\Delta$ multiplet, the order is reversed from the nucleon doublet: $\begin{pmatrix} \Delta^{++} \\ \Delta^{+} \\ \Delta^{0} \\ \Delta^{-} \end{pmatrix}$.

  • Generalized Gell-MannâNishijima Formula (Quark Picture):

    • To account for new flavors like Charm (C), Bottomness (B'), and Topness (T), the formula is extended: $$ Q = I_3 + \frac{B+S+C+B'+T}{2} $$
    • Quark Charges:
      • Up-type quarks (u, c, t): $Q = +2/3 e$.
      • Down-type quarks (d, s, b): $Q = -1/3 e$.
    • Quark Composition of Hadrons:
      • A proton is (uud). $Q = 2/3 + 2/3 - 1/3 = 1$. $B=1/3+1/3+1/3=1$. $I_3 = 1/2(n_u-n_{\bar{u}}) - 1/2(n_d-n_{\bar{d}}) = 1/2(2) - 1/2(1) = 1/2$.
      • A neutron is (udd). $Q = 2/3 - 1/3 - 1/3 = 0$. $B=1$. $I_3 = 1/2(1) - 1/2(2) = -1/2$.
    • Strange Mesons: Kaons ($K^+, K^0, \dots$) are strange mesons, containing a strange quark or anti-quark. Example: $K^+$ is $(u\bar{s})$. $S=+1$.
    • Strangeness Definition for Quarks: $S = -(n_s - n_{\bar{s}})$. (Usually, strange quark $s$ has $S=-1$, anti-strange $\bar{s}$ has $S=+1$).
    • Charm: $C = n_c - n_{\bar{c}}$.
    • Bottomness: $B' = n_b - n_{\bar{b}}$.
    • Baryon Number for Quarks: $B = \frac{1}{3}(n_q - n_{\bar{q}})$.

Lecture 26: November 16, 2023 - Delta Baryon Decays & Exam Review

Lecture 26: November 16, 2023, Thursday

  • Delta Baryons (I=3/2):

    • The $\Delta$ quadruplet consists of $\Delta^{++}$ (uuu), $\Delta^{+}$ (uud), $\Delta^{0}$ (udd), $\Delta^{-}$ (ddd).
    • These have the same quark content as (p, n) but different isospin (and higher mass). $M_\Delta \approx 1232 \text{ MeV}$.

  • Decay Modes and Isospin Conservation:

    • $\Delta$ baryons decay via the strong interaction (e.g., $\Delta^{++} \rightarrow p^+ + \pi^+$, $\Delta^{+} \rightarrow p^+ + \pi^0$ or $n + \pi^+$, $\Delta^{0} \rightarrow n + \pi^0$ or $p^+ + \pi^-$, $\Delta^{-} \rightarrow n + \pi^-$).
    • Since these are strong decays, isospin ($I$) and its third component ($I_3$) are conserved.
    • This implies that the total decay rates (lifetimes) of all members of an isospin multiplet should be the same.
    • Branching Ratios: The relative probabilities of decay into different final states are determined by Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, which describe how isospins couple.
      • Example: For $\Delta^+ \rightarrow p\pi^0$ vs $n\pi^+$.
        • $\Delta^+$ has $I=3/2, I_3=1/2$.
        • $p$ has $I=1/2, I_3=1/2$. $\pi^0$ has $I=1, I_3=0$.
        • $n$ has $I=1/2, I_3=-1/2$. $\pi^+$ has $I=1, I_3=1$.
      • The decay rates are proportional to the square of the Clebsch-Gordan coefficients. The notes show $\frac{\Gamma(\Delta^{++} \rightarrow p\pi^+)}{\Gamma(\Delta^{+} \rightarrow n\pi^+)} = 3$.

  • Rho Meson (I=1):

    • The $\rho$ meson triplet ($\rho^+, \rho^0, \rho^-$) decays to two pions (e.g., $\rho^+ \rightarrow \pi^+ + \pi^0$).
    • $\rho^0$ can decay to $\pi^+ + \pi^-$ or $\pi^0 + \pi^0$.
    • The decay $\rho^0 \rightarrow \pi^0 + \pi^0$ is "experimentally not obtained." This is due to conservation laws like G-parity and isospin. For two identical bosons like two $\pi^0$s, the total wave function must be symmetric under exchange. Since $\pi^0$ is a boson, $I=1$ coupling to two identical pions must be antisymmetric, which is not possible for an identical boson final state.
